LEC 11
Deterministic vs. Probabilistic Models
Language First

A deterministic model gives one exact output for each input. Same input, same output, every time. No randomness. "Distance = speed × time" is deterministic — 60 km/h for 2 hours is always exactly 120 km.

A probabilistic model gives a range of possible outputs with likelihoods. "A person who smokes 20 cigarettes a day has a 30% probability of lung cancer by age 60." Same input, different possible outcomes — it produces a distribution, not a single answer.

Deterministic: y = f(x) — one x gives exactly one y
Probabilistic: P(Y|X=x) — given x, what is the probability distribution of Y?
The Translation Question PU Will Ask

"Every person exposed to the chemical develops the disease" — deterministic (absolute claim). "A person has a 60% chance of developing the disease" — probabilistic. Casual language blurs this. Detect which model the claim implies before answering.

Mechanics & Notation
Deterministic test: does the same input always produce the identical output, with zero variation? If yes, deterministic.
Probabilistic test: does the claim involve a rate, percentage, "typically," "tends to," or any language admitting exceptions? If yes, probabilistic.
Mixed claims: a deterministic rule can govern a probabilistic input — e.g. "if convicted (deterministic legal consequence), sentence length follows a probabilistic distribution based on prior record."

The keyword test is fast but not foolproof — "always" can appear in a probabilistic claim used loosely ("violent offenders are always considered higher risk"), so the real test is whether the claim, taken literally and precisely, logically admits exceptions. If it does, even one, it's probabilistic regardless of how confident the wording sounds.

Worked Example
Classifying a Mixed Statutory Claim

"Any person who drives with a blood alcohol level above the legal limit is guilty of an offence. Studies show such drivers are involved in collisions at roughly 4 times the rate of sober drivers."

First sentence: deterministic. "Is guilty" admits no exceptions once the antecedent (above the limit) is satisfied — this is a legal rule, y = f(x) with one fixed output.

Second sentence: probabilistic. "Roughly 4 times the rate" is a statistical tendency drawn from a distribution of outcomes across many drivers — it says nothing certain about any individual driver.

A single paragraph can mix both model types, and the skill is classifying each claim independently rather than letting the confident tone of one infect your read of the other.

Practice Questions
T2 · AClassify each as deterministic or probabilistic: (a) "If a contract is breached, the innocent party is entitled to damages." (b) "Companies that cut R&D spending typically see declining profits within 3 years." (c) "Water boils at 100°C at sea level."

(a) Deterministic. Legal entitlement follows from breach as a matter of rule — no probability involved.

(b) Probabilistic. "Typically" signals a statistical tendency. Some companies will not follow the pattern.

(c) Deterministic. Physical law under specified conditions — same input always produces the same output.

Classification · legal rules as deterministic · statistical tendencies as probabilistic
T2 · BAn expert witness testifies: "In my experience, defendants who show remorse receive lighter sentences." Deterministic or probabilistic? How should a lawyer challenge this in cross-examination?

Probabilistic. "In my experience" and absence of absolute language marks this as a statistical claim.

Cross-examination: Ask the expert to quantify — what percentage of cases? What sample size? What is the base rate of lighter sentences without remorse shown? Without quantification, the claim cannot be evaluated. A confident-sounding probabilistic claim is significantly weaker evidence than the jury may assume.

Probabilistic expert testimony · demanding quantification · gap between statistical tendency and legal rule
T3 · AA risk model predicts: "Facilities with safety scores below 60 have a 78% probability of a serious incident within 5 years." Facility X scores 55 and has had no incident. The regulator wants to close it. The facility argues: "Probability is not certainty." Analyse both positions using the deterministic/probabilistic distinction.

The facility is logically correct but strategically weak. The model is explicitly probabilistic — 0.78, not 1.0. No incident to date is consistent with being in the 22% that don't experience incidents. The facility correctly notes probability is not certainty.

The regulator's stronger point: Regulation is anticipatory. Requiring a deterministic outcome (an actual incident) before acting defeats the purpose of probabilistic risk models entirely. The relevant question is what threshold of probability justifies preventive intervention — which is a policy question, not a logical one.

The logical tension: The facility applies deterministic reasoning (no incident = no violation) to a probabilistic regulatory framework. The resolution depends on whether the regulation itself specifies a probability threshold for intervention, or uses deterministic language ("shall be closed if score below 60").

Probabilistic vs. deterministic regulatory frameworks · anticipatory regulation · applying the correct model type to the relevant framework
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Litigation Connection

Expert reports routinely present probabilistic findings using language that sounds deterministic — a lawyer who can't separate "this risk factor is associated with a 30% increase" from "this risk factor causes the outcome" will either overstate or understate the report's actual evidential weight in cross-examination.

Regulatory and sentencing frameworks frequently mix both model types in a single provision — a deterministic legal consequence (guilt, liability) triggered by an underlying probabilistic risk assessment. Spotting where the rule shifts from "if this exact fact, then this consequence" to "if this statistical threshold, then this consequence" is often the precise point where a regulation can be challenged as arbitrary or under-specified.

LEC 12
Linear Functions & Real-World Modeling
Language First

A linear function describes a relationship where change is constant. For every additional unit of input, the output changes by the same fixed amount. The graph is a straight line.

y = mx + c
m = slope = rate of change (how much y changes per unit of x)
c = y-intercept = value of y when x = 0 (the starting point)

Slope from two points: m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)

In plain language: a lawyer who charges a fixed consultation fee plus an hourly rate has a linear total fee. Hourly rate = m. Fixed fee = c. Hours worked = x.

The Word Problem Translation Step

PU describes a situation in prose. First move: identify what is constant per period (slope m) and what is fixed regardless of x (intercept c). Never write an equation before you have identified both.

Mechanics & Notation
Building a model from prose, step by step:
1. Find the fixed amount (appears regardless of usage) → c
2. Find the per-unit rate (multiplied by usage) → m
3. Identify the input variable and what it counts → x
4. Assemble: y = mx + c

Solving for x given y: y = mx + c → x = (y − c) / m

Word problems frequently state the fixed and variable amounts in either order, or bury the fixed amount mid-sentence rather than at the start — the mechanical fix is to extract both numbers first, label them m and c explicitly, and only then write the equation, rather than trying to write the equation directly from the prose in one pass.

Worked Example
Building the Model

"A law firm charges a fixed registration fee of Rs. 5,000 plus Rs. 2,000 per hour."

c = 5,000 (fixed). m = 2,000 (per hour). Model: Fee = 2,000x + 5,000.

At 3 hours: 2,000(3) + 5,000 = Rs. 11,000. For Rs. 15,000 budget: 15,000 = 2,000x + 5,000 → x = 5 hours.

Practice Questions
T2 · AA court imposes a fine of Rs. 10,000 plus Rs. 500 for each day a violation continues. Write the linear model. What is the fine after 30 days? How many days until the fine reaches Rs. 35,000?
F(d) = 500d + 10,000
After 30 days: F(30) = 15,000 + 10,000 = Rs. 25,000
When F = 35,000: 35,000 = 500d + 10,000 → d = 50 days
Linear model construction · evaluation · solving for x given y
T2 · BProvider A: Rs. 3,000 fixed + Rs. 1,500/hour. Provider B: no fixed fee, Rs. 2,000/hour. At what hours do they cost the same? Which is cheaper for a 4-hour job? A 10-hour job?
A(h) = 1,500h + 3,000 · B(h) = 2,000h
Equal: 1,500h + 3,000 = 2,000h → h = 6 hours
At h=4: A=9,000, B=8,000 → B cheaper
At h=10: A=18,000, B=20,000 → A cheaper

B cheaper under 6 hours. A cheaper over 6 hours. The crossover is the break-even point.

Two linear functions · break-even · comparative cost analysis
T3 · AA damages model assumes harm grows linearly at Rs. 50,000/month from breach. Actual data: Rs. 80,000 in month 1, Rs. 110,000 in month 2. Is the linear model appropriate? What does the data suggest, and what is the legal significance?
Linear model at Rs. 50,000/month predicts:
Month 1: 50,000 · Month 2: 100,000
Actual: Month 1: 80,000 · Month 2: 110,000
Actual month-on-month increment: 30,000 (not 50,000)
Month 1 actual (80,000) exceeds prediction (50,000) — initial losses understated

Model is not appropriate. The assumed rate of Rs. 50,000/month is inconsistent with the data — actual month 1 losses are 60% higher. The increment (Rs. 30,000) also differs from the assumed rate, suggesting either a different linear rate or a non-linear pattern.

Legal significance: If the correct rate is higher than modelled, the plaintiff is undercompensated. The direction of error likely favours the plaintiff, not the defendant. The defendant's challenge to the model may inadvertently expose a larger damages figure.

Testing linear model against data · comparing predicted vs. actual · legal consequence of model error direction
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Billing & Damages Connection

Linear fee structures (retainer plus hourly, or fixed plus per-unit damages) are the default model in legal billing and in many statutory damages schemes — a lawyer who can instantly extract m and c from a fee agreement or a damages clause can answer "what will this cost at X hours" or "at what point does Provider A become cheaper than Provider B" without needing a calculator mid-negotiation.

Testing a linear damages model against actual data, as in the T3 question above, is exactly the analysis a damages expert performs when a defendant disputes a plaintiff's claimed rate of harm — and recognising which direction a modelling error favours (under- or over-compensation) is a recurring, high-value litigation skill.

LEC 12
Interpreting Slope & Intercept
Language First

Slope (m) answers: "How fast is this changing?" — the rate of change per unit of x.

Intercept (c) answers: "What is the baseline?" — the value when x is zero.

Positive slope: y increases as x increases — growth
Negative slope: y decreases as x increases — decay or reduction
Zero slope: y is constant — no relationship with x
Mechanics & Notation
Reading slope from prose: look for "per," "each," "every," "rate of" — the number attached to one of these words is m, and its sign comes from whether the quantity is rising or falling.
Reading intercept from prose: look for "starting," "initial," "at the outset," "before any [x] occurred" — this is the value at x=0.
Comparing two models: set them equal to find the crossover point; check which has the larger |slope| to determine which changes faster long-term, regardless of which starts higher.

A model with a small intercept but large slope will eventually overtake a model with a large intercept but small slope — the crossover point is exactly where m₁x + c₁ = m₂x + c₂, solved for x.

Worked Example
Interpreting and Comparing Two Models

"Compliance costs for Firm A start at Rs. 200,000 and rise by Rs. 40,000 per year. Compliance costs for Firm B start at Rs. 350,000 and rise by Rs. 15,000 per year."

Firm A: c = 200,000, m = 40,000 → A(t) = 40,000t + 200,000. Firm B: c = 350,000, m = 15,000 → B(t) = 15,000t + 350,000.

Crossover: 40,000t + 200,000 = 15,000t + 350,000 → 25,000t = 150,000 → t = 6 years.

Before year 6, Firm B has higher costs (larger intercept). After year 6, Firm A overtakes — its steeper slope eventually dominates the smaller starting gap. A firm choosing between the two structures needs to know its planning horizon, not just which number looks smaller today.

Practice Questions
T2 · APollution complaints: C = -15t + 300, where t = years since new regulation. Interpret the slope and intercept in plain English. When will complaints reach zero?
m = -15: complaints decrease by 15 per year
c = 300: at t=0, there were 300 complaints per year
When C=0: 0 = -15t + 300 → t = 20 years

At this rate, complaints reach zero in 20 years. In reality, complaints may plateau above zero — the linear model is valid within the range where its assumptions hold.

Slope as rate of change · intercept as baseline · solving for y=0 · model limitations
T3 · ATwo models for a river's pollution: Model A: P = 8t + 20 (rising). Model B: P = -3t + 95 (falling). Safety threshold: P = 60. Under each model, when does the river cross the threshold? At t=5, which model gives higher pollution? What information resolves which model to rely on?
Model A unsafe when P > 60: 8t+20=60 → t=5 years
Model B currently: P(0)=95 — already unsafe
Model B safe when: -3t+95=60 → t=11.7 years
At t=5: A = 8(5)+20 = 60 · B = -3(5)+95 = 80
Model B gives higher pollution at t=5

Models give contradictory accounts — A shows rising from low base, B shows falling from high base. The regulator cannot use both.

Information needed: A single current measurement resolves it immediately — A predicts 20 units today, B predicts 95 units today. One measurement distinguishes them. Historical trend data validates the slope. The cause of the trend (industrial growth vs. cleanup programme) determines which direction is correct.

Legal implication: Model A says the river is at the threshold now and worsening. Model B says it is currently unsafe but improving. These call for opposite interventions. Model selection is not a technicality — it determines the entire regulatory response.

Competing linear models · threshold crossing in both directions · single data point disambiguation · legal consequence of model selection
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Regulatory Strategy Connection

Two competing linear models making opposite directional claims about the same regulated quantity — exactly the river-pollution scenario above — is a recurring fact pattern in environmental and public-health litigation, where opposing experts each fit a line to the same underlying (often noisy) data and reach contradictory conclusions about trend direction. The cross-examination move is always the same: demand a single current, verifiable data point, since that alone discriminates between two lines that agree nowhere except possibly by coincidence.

Crossover-point analysis — at what point does Option A become better than Option B — is the standard structure behind cost-benefit arguments in regulatory compliance disputes and in advising clients on competing contractual fee structures.

Lec. 14–16 · Systems of Linear Equations
LEC 14
Systems of Linear Equations
Language First

A system of linear equations requires two or more conditions to be satisfied simultaneously. Geometrically: each equation is a line — the solution is where they cross.

One solution: lines cross at one point — consistent, independent
No solution: lines are parallel — inconsistent
Infinite solutions: lines are identical — dependent

Substitution: solve one equation for one variable, substitute
Elimination: multiply to match coefficients, then add/subtract
Translation Step — Always First

Define variables explicitly before writing equations. "Let x = ... and y = ..." written down forces the translation and prevents setup errors. Never skip this step.

Mechanics & Notation
Elimination procedure:
1. Multiply one or both equations so a variable's coefficients match (or are opposite)
2. Add (if opposite signs) or subtract (if same sign) to cancel that variable
3. Solve the resulting single-variable equation
4. Substitute back into either original equation to find the second variable
5. Check both original equations with both values

Substitution is faster when one equation already isolates a variable cleanly; elimination is faster when both equations are in standard form (ax + by = c) with no variable pre-isolated. Word problems rarely tell you which to use — recognising the equations' shape after translation is what determines the efficient method, not a rule to memorise in isolation.

Worked Example
Solving by Elimination

"A settlement fund pays Party X and Party Y. Three times Party X's payment plus Party Y's payment totals Rs. 280,000. Party X's payment minus Party Y's payment is Rs. 40,000."

Let X = Party X payment, Y = Party Y payment
Eq.1: 3X + Y = 280,000
Eq.2: X − Y = 40,000

Add Eq.1 + Eq.2 (Y cancels): 4X = 320,000 → X = 80,000
Substitute into Eq.2: 80,000 − Y = 40,000 → Y = 40,000
Check Eq.1: 3(80,000) + 40,000 = 280,000 ✓

Party X receives Rs. 80,000, Party Y receives Rs. 40,000 — elimination was faster here than substitution because both equations were already in a form where adding them directly cancelled Y.

Practice Questions
T2 · ATwo parties in a dispute each claim a share of a Rs. 500,000 fund. Party A claims twice what Party B claims. Both claims total Rs. 500,000. Find each party's claim. If a mediator splits the fund equally, what does each party receive?
Let A = Party A claim, B = Party B claim
A = 2B · A + B = 500,000
2B + B = 500,000 → B = 166,667 · A = 333,333
Equal split: each receives Rs. 250,000

Party A accepts Rs. 83,333 less than claimed. Party B receives Rs. 83,333 more than claimed. The equal split clearly favours the weaker claimant.

Two-variable system · substitution · interpreting the solution in a dispute context
T2 · BA car travels 200 km partly at 40 km/h and partly at 60 km/h. Total time: 4 hours. Find the distance at each speed. (This is the QR-I past paper Q2-ii — the disguised systems question.)
Let d₁ = distance at 40 km/h · d₂ = distance at 60 km/h
Eq.1 (distance): d₁ + d₂ = 200
Eq.2 (time): d₁/40 + d₂/60 = 4

From Eq.1: d₁ = 200 - d₂
Substitute: (200-d₂)/40 + d₂/60 = 4
Multiply by 120: 3(200-d₂) + 2d₂ = 480
600 - d₂ = 480 → d₂ = 120 km · d₁ = 80 km
Check: 80/40 + 120/60 = 2 + 2 = 4 hours ✓
Distance-time word problem as system · the translation step that stumped QR-I students · time = distance/speed as second equation
T3 · ATwo companies set prices: P_A = 3Q + 10 and P_B = -Q + 50 (Q = thousands of units). At what quantity do they charge the same price? A regulator argues: "If both firms are at this quantity, they are coordinating." Is this logically valid? What additional evidence would be needed?
3Q + 10 = -Q + 50 → 4Q = 40 → Q = 10
Price at Q=10: P = 3(10)+10 = 40 ✓ Check: -10+50=40 ✓

The regulator's logic is not valid as stated. Equal prices at the same quantity is the natural market equilibrium — the intersection of their pricing formulas. In a competitive market, firms naturally converge to this point.

Additional evidence needed: (1) Evidence one firm communicated its pricing formula to the other — coincident outcomes do not prove communication. (2) Evidence both firms departed from what their independent cost structures would suggest. (3) Suspicious timing — sudden simultaneous convergence rather than gradual market adjustment. The logical error is post hoc: coincident pricing at equilibrium does not imply coordination caused the coincidence.

System intersection as market equilibrium · correlation vs. causation in regulatory enforcement · what constitutes evidence of collusion beyond price similarity
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Antitrust & Settlement Connection

The pricing-intersection scenario above is the actual structure of "parallel pricing" antitrust disputes — courts consistently hold that identical or converging prices alone, without independent evidence of an agreement, do not establish collusion, precisely because competitive markets naturally produce price convergence at equilibrium. Knowing where that intersection point sits mathematically, and that it's an expected market outcome rather than a tell of misconduct, sharpens an antitrust defence considerably.

Multi-party settlement allocation — splitting a fund according to two or more stated conditions — is routinely a two-variable system in practice, and a lawyer who can set up and solve it directly avoids relying on a mediator's potentially self-serving framing of "fair" division.

LEC 15
Linear Inequalities & Their Applications
Language First

An inequality describes a range of acceptable values. Legal thresholds, regulatory limits, and damage caps are inequalities in disguise.

Solving rule — the one that trips everyone:
When you multiply or divide both sides by a NEGATIVE number,
the inequality sign FLIPS direction.

2x > 10 → x > 5 (divided by +2, unchanged)
-2x > 10 → x < -5 (divided by -2, FLIPS)

Build the habit: every time you divide or multiply by a negative, explicitly pause, check, and flip.

Mechanics & Notation
Compound inequality: a ≤ x ≤ b means BOTH x ≥ a AND x ≤ b must hold simultaneously — never just one
Quadratic inequality procedure: 1. Move everything to one side (≤ 0 or ≥ 0 form) 2. Factor 3. Find the roots 4. Determine the sign of the parabola between/outside the roots based on whether it opens up or down
Sign chart shortcut: for (x−r₁)(x−r₂), the expression is negative between the roots and positive outside them, when the parabola opens upward

The single most exam-relevant fact about compound inequalities: a value satisfying only one half of a two-sided constraint has not satisfied the constraint at all — this is the same ∀ vs. ∃ distinction from Part 1, now showing up inside ordinary algebra rather than formal logic notation.

Worked Example
Solving a Quadratic Inequality Step by Step

Solve: x² - 2x - 15 ≥ 0

Already in ≥ 0 form.
Factor: (x-5)(x+3) ≥ 0
Roots: x = 5, x = -3
Parabola opens upward (positive x² coefficient) → expression is POSITIVE outside the roots, negative between them
Since we want ≥ 0 (positive or zero): x ≤ -3 OR x ≥ 5

Check with a test point in each region: x=0 (between roots) → (0-5)(0+3) = -15, negative — correctly excluded. x=6 (outside, right) → (6-5)(6+3)=9, positive — correctly included. The test-point check is the fast way to confirm a sign chart without re-deriving the parabola's shape from scratch.

Practice Questions
T2 · ACompliance budget cannot exceed Rs. 500,000. Fixed costs Rs. 200,000. Variable cost Rs. 15,000 per audit. Write the inequality and find the maximum audits. Also solve: 7x + 3 ≤ 3x + 11 and x² - 7x - 30 ≤ 0.
15,000a + 200,000 ≤ 500,000 → a ≤ 20 audits

7x + 3 ≤ 3x + 11 → 4x ≤ 8 → x ≤ 2

x² - 7x - 30 ≤ 0
Factor: (x-10)(x+3) ≤ 0
Parabola opens upward → negative between roots
Solution: -3 ≤ x ≤ 10
Word problem to inequality · linear inequality · quadratic inequality via factoring · direct QR-I past paper type
T3 · ASentencing guideline: 24 ≤ T ≤ 120 months for a category B offence. A judge imposes T=18 citing mitigating circumstances. The prosecution says this violates the guideline. The defence argues T=18 satisfies -T + 120 ≥ 0. Analyse all three mathematical claims.
Guideline: 24 ≤ T ≤ 120 (compound inequality — BOTH must hold)
T = 18: Is 18 ≥ 24? NO. Lower bound violated.
Is 18 ≤ 120? Yes. Upper bound satisfied.
Result: VIOLATES the guideline — lower bound not met.

Defence claim: -T + 120 ≥ 0 simplifies to T ≤ 120.
T=18 satisfies this — technically correct.
But this is only the upper bound. The lower bound is omitted.

The prosecution is correct. T=18 falls below the mandatory minimum of 24.

The defence's error: It checked only half the compound inequality and presented that partial satisfaction as if it meant full compliance. Formally, the defence substituted ∃-satisfaction (satisfying one condition) for ∀-satisfaction (satisfying all conditions simultaneously). This maps directly back to Part 1 — a compound constraint requires all its elements to be met, not just some.

Compound inequality · two-sided constraint evaluation · selective presentation of mathematical truth · connection to ∀ vs. ∃ from Part 1
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Sentencing & Compliance Connection

Sentencing guidelines, regulatory thresholds, and statutory damage caps are compound inequalities in the overwhelming majority of real statutory drafting — "not less than X and not more than Y" is literally a ≤ T ≤ b written in prose. The defence's error in the worked example above (checking only one bound and presenting it as full compliance) is a genuinely common, exploitable advocacy mistake, and spotting it instantly is a direct payoff of this exact skill.

On the LSAT, "necessary but not sufficient" reasoning errors map onto exactly this pattern — satisfying one half of a two-part requirement is frequently presented as if it satisfies the whole, and the correct answer choice in a Flaw question often names precisely this gap.

LEC 16
Introduction to Linear Programming
Language First

Linear programming answers: "Given constraints, what is the best possible outcome?" It optimises an objective function subject to inequality constraints.

1. Decision variables: what you are choosing (x, y)
2. Objective function: what to maximise or minimise
3. Constraints: the limitations (budget, capacity, regulatory limits)

The optimal solution always occurs at a corner point of the feasible region.

A regulator allocating inspection resources, a law firm deciding how to allocate associate time across cases, a company maximising output within environmental permit limits — all are linear programming problems.

Mechanics & Notation
Corner point method, step by step:
1. Graph every constraint inequality
2. Identify the feasible region — the area satisfying all constraints simultaneously
3. Find every corner (vertex) of that region by solving pairs of constraint equations together
4. Evaluate the objective function at every corner
5. The maximum (or minimum) always occurs at one of those corners — never in the interior, never on an edge's midpoint

The corner-point guarantee is what makes linear programming tractable by hand: instead of checking infinitely many points in the feasible region, you only ever need to check a small, finite list of vertices. Missing a corner is the single most common source of a wrong "optimal" answer — always count the corners systematically rather than eyeballing the graph for the best-looking point.

Worked Example
Full LP Setup and Corner Point Evaluation

A firm produces two products, P and Q. Each unit of P needs 2 hours labour and yields Rs. 30 profit. Each unit of Q needs 4 hours labour and yields Rs. 50 profit. Available labour: 40 hours. Maximum 15 units of P can be sold (market cap).

Maximise Z = 30P + 50Q
Subject to: 2P + 4Q ≤ 40  (labour)
        P ≤ 15  (market cap)
        P, Q ≥ 0

Finding the corners: (0,0): Z=0. (15,0): labour used = 30 ≤ 40, feasible, Z=450. (0,10): from 2(0)+4Q=40, Z=500. (15,2.5): intersection of P=15 and 2(15)+4Q=40 → Q=2.5, Z=30(15)+50(2.5)=575.

Optimal: P=15, Q=2.5, Z=575. Notice the highest-profit corner isn't the one using all labour on the highest per-unit-profit product (Q) alone — it's the intersection of two binding constraints together, which is exactly why checking every corner systematically matters more than reasoning informally about which product "seems" better.

Practice Questions
T2 · AA legal aid office handles civil (C) and criminal (Cr) cases. Civil: 3 hours, 4 impact points. Criminal: 5 hours, 7 impact points. Available: 60 hours/week. Write the LP and find what combination maximises impact points.
Maximise Z = 4C + 7Cr
Subject to: 3C + 5Cr ≤ 60 · C,Cr ≥ 0

Corner points:
(0,0): Z=0 · (20,0): Z=80 · (0,12): Z=84

Maximum: Z=84 at (0,12) — 12 criminal cases, 0 civil

Criminal cases generate more impact per hour (7/5=1.4) than civil (4/3=1.33). The office maximises impact by focusing entirely on criminal cases given the time constraint.

T3 · AA regulator allocates teams between factory inspections (F: Rs. 8,000, 2 days, prevents 3 incidents) and river monitoring (R: Rs. 5,000, 1 day, prevents 2 incidents). Budget: Rs. 80,000. Time: 25 days. Formulate the LP, find optimal allocation, and explain what changes if the budget is cut to Rs. 60,000.
Maximise Z = 3F + 2R
Subject to: 8,000F + 5,000R ≤ 80,000
2F + R ≤ 25 · F,R ≥ 0

Corner points (within both constraints):
(0,0): Z=0 · (10,0): Z=30 · (0,16): Z=32
Optimal: F=0, R=16 → Z=32 incidents prevented

At Rs. 60,000 budget: (0,12): Z=24
Optimal unchanged — still all river visits

Budget cut from Rs. 80,000 to Rs. 60,000 reduces optimal prevention from 32 to 24 incidents — 25% reduction for 25% budget cut. River visits remain optimal at both levels: 2/5,000 = 0.0004 impact per rupee vs. 3/8,000 = 0.000375 for factory inspections.

NY Bar · Resource Allocation Connection

Regulatory agencies, legal aid organisations, and law firms routinely face exactly this optimisation problem — limited hours, limited budget, multiple case types or compliance activities competing for the same constrained resources. A lawyer who can formalise "maximise impact subject to a budget and time constraint" as an actual LP, rather than allocating by instinct or precedent, brings a genuinely persuasive, defensible basis to resource-allocation arguments in budget hearings or amicus submissions on agency rulemaking.

Lec. 17–18 · Linear vs. Exponential Growth & Decay
LEC 17
Linear vs. Exponential Growth — The Core Distinction
Language First — The Single Most Important Distinction in Part 3

Linear growth: you add the same amount each period. Like saving Rs. 1,000 every month — each month the total increases by a fixed amount.

Exponential growth: you multiply by the same factor each period. Like a bank account growing at 10% per year — each year the increase is larger than the last because you are earning interest on a bigger balance.

The difference in plain language: linear adds, exponential multiplies.

Linear: y = mt + c — add m every period
Exponential: y = a · bᵗ — multiply by b every period
where a = initial value, b = growth factor

Growth factor b:
b > 1 → growth (e.g. b=1.15 means 15% increase per period)
0 < b < 1 → decay (e.g. b=0.80 means 20% decrease per period)
The QR-I Killer — Now in QR-II

This is the exact category that produced the base-conversion question in QR-I. PU will give you a word problem and you must decide: does this situation add a fixed amount (linear) or multiply by a fixed factor (exponential)? Salary increase by Rs. 5,000/year — linear. Salary increase by 8% per year — exponential. Population growing by 200 people/year — linear. Population growing at 3% per year — exponential. The signal words: "by X per period" (linear) vs. "by X% per period" or "grows at a rate of" (exponential).

Mechanics & Key Formulas
Exponential growth: A = P(1 + r)ᵗ
P = initial value · r = rate (as decimal) · t = time periods

Exponential decay: A = P(1 - r)ᵗ

Continuous growth (natural e): A = Peʳᵗ
Used when growth is continuous rather than periodic

Doubling time (Rule of 70):
Approximate years to double = 70 / (rate as percentage)
At 7% growth: doubles in ≈ 10 years

Identifying the Model Before Computing

"A fine schedule increases by 8% of the current fine for each week of continued violation, starting from Rs. 50,000." Find the fine after 6 weeks.

Step 1 — identify the model: "8% of the current fine" is a percentage of an already-changing base — exponential, not linear. The trap here is that a careless reader sees a single number (8%) and a starting value and reaches for A = P + rt out of habit; the percentage-of-current-value structure is the signal that overrides that instinct.

A = P(1 + r)ᵗ = 50,000(1.08)⁶
(1.08)⁶ ≈ 1.5869
A ≈ 50,000 × 1.5869 ≈ Rs. 79,345

Step 2 — sanity-check against the wrong linear answer: if mistakenly treated as linear at 8% of the original 50,000 per week (Rs. 4,000/week), the answer would be 50,000 + 6(4,000) = Rs. 74,000 — close enough to the correct figure to look plausible, but wrong, and the gap widens every additional week. This is exactly the kind of near-miss PU exploits, because a linear error here doesn't produce an obviously absurd number.

T3 · A Two environmental contamination models are proposed for a site: Model L (linear): C(t) = -8t + 100. Model E (exponential): C(t) = 100 × (0.92)ᵗ. Both start at C=100 (units of contamination) at t=0. A safety threshold is C=20. Using each model, find when the site becomes safe. At t=5, which model gives higher contamination? What does the difference between models mean for a remediation timeline presented to a court?
Model L: C = 20 when -8t + 100 = 20 → 8t = 80 → t = 10 years

Model E: C = 20 when 100(0.92)ᵗ = 20
(0.92)ᵗ = 0.20
t·ln(0.92) = ln(0.20)
t = ln(0.20)/ln(0.92) = -1.6094 / -0.0834 ≈ 19.3 years

At t = 5:
Model L: C = -8(5)+100 = 60
Model E: C = 100(0.92)⁵ = 100(0.659) = 65.9

Model E gives higher contamination at t=5 (65.9 vs. 60)

What the difference means for a remediation timeline: The linear model predicts safety in 10 years. The exponential model predicts safety in ~19 years — nearly double. The exponential model's decay is faster initially (steep early decline) but slows as contamination levels decrease — the percentage reduction is constant but the absolute reduction shrinks each year. The linear model assumes constant absolute reduction regardless of remaining contamination level.

Court implications: If the defendant's expert uses the linear model and the plaintiff's expert uses the exponential, they will give dramatically different remediation timelines to the court. The choice of model is not a technical detail — it determines whether the site is safe in 10 or 19 years, which directly affects damages (loss of use of land), injunctive relief duration, and remediation cost estimates. The court would need to determine which model is empirically supported by contamination data from similar sites.

LEC 17
Exponential Equations & Base Conversion
Language First — The QR-I Killer, Addressed Directly

An exponential equation is one where the variable appears in the exponent: 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1). You cannot solve this by standard algebra because x is not multiplied — it is in the power position.

The technique: convert both sides to the same base, then set the exponents equal.

Why does this work? If bᵃ = bᶜ, then a = c — equal powers of the same base must have equal exponents. This is only usable once both sides share the same base.

The hidden step: Before any algebra, ask —
"Can both sides be written as powers of the same number?"

4 = 2² and 8 = 2³ → both are powers of 2
This is the recognition step. Miss it and the equation is unsolvable by this method.
This Is Exactly What Stumped QR-I

The question 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1) cannot be solved by treating it like a linear equation. The trap is starting to manipulate x without first recognising that 4 and 8 are both powers of 2. The recognition step comes before any algebra. Build the habit: see an exponential equation — ask "what is the common base?" before writing anything else.

Mechanics — Full Worked Solution
Solving 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1) — The QR-I Question

Step 1 — Recognise the common base: 4 = 2², 8 = 2³. Both are powers of 2.

Step 2 — Rewrite both sides:

(2²)^(x+5) = (2³)^(x-1)

Step 3 — Apply power rule (multiply exponents):

2^(2(x+5)) = 2^(3(x-1))

Step 4 — Same base, set exponents equal:

2(x+5) = 3(x-1)
2x + 10 = 3x - 3
10 + 3 = 3x - 2x
x = 13

Verify: 4^18 = (2²)^18 = 2^36. 8^12 = (2³)^12 = 2^36. ✓

Common base pairs to know:
4 = 2² · 8 = 2³ · 16 = 2⁴ · 32 = 2⁵
9 = 3² · 27 = 3³ · 81 = 3⁴
25 = 5² · 125 = 5³
If no common integer base exists → use logarithms
Practice Questions

Step 1: Common base — 9 = 3², 27 = 3³
Step 2: (3²)^(x-1) = (3³)^(x+2)
Step 3: 3^(2(x-1)) = 3^(3(x+2))
Step 4: 2(x-1) = 3(x+2)
2x - 2 = 3x + 6
-2 - 6 = 3x - 2x
x = -8

Verify: 9^(-9) = (3²)^(-9) = 3^(-18)
27^(-6) = (3³)^(-6) = 3^(-18) ✓

Step 2: (2⁴)^(2x+1) = (2⁵)^(x-1)
Step 3: 2^(4(2x+1)) = 2^(5(x-1))

T3 · A A forensic accountant models fraudulent asset growth as A₁ = 4^(t+2) and compares it to a legitimate benchmark B₁ = 8^t. At what time t do they coincide? What does the intersection mean financially, and what happens to the ratio A₁/B₁ as t increases beyond the intersection?
Set 4^(t+2) = 8^t
Common base: 4=2², 8=2³
(2²)^(t+2) = (2³)^t
2^(2t+4) = 2^(3t)
2t + 4 = 3t
t = 4

At t=4 they coincide. Verify: 4^6 = 4096. 8^4 = 4096. ✓

Financial meaning: At t=4, the fraudulent asset value equals the legitimate benchmark. Before t=4, compare: at t=0, A₁=4²=16, B₁=8⁰=1 — the fraud starts far above the benchmark. The fraud appears to outperform legitimately early on. At t=4 they converge. After t=4, B₁=8^t grows faster (base 8 > base 4 after conversion — effectively 2³ per period vs. 2² per period for the exponent increment).

Ratio A₁/B₁ after t=4: A₁/B₁ = 2^(2t+4)/2^(3t) = 2^(4-t). For t>4, this is 2^(negative) = fraction less than 1. So after t=4, the legitimate benchmark overtakes the fraudulent model — the fraud's inflated early returns are unsustainable and the gap inverts. This is the mathematical signature of a Ponzi-like structure: early apparent outperformance followed by inevitable underperformance relative to legitimate growth.

A lawyer who can set up and solve the intersection of two exponential models can independently verify (or challenge) an expert's claim about when a fraudulent scheme's "outperformance" was bound to collapse.

LEC 18
Rate of Change & Introduction to Derivatives
Language First

The rate of change answers: "How fast is this changing right now?" For a linear function, the rate of change is constant — it is the slope m, the same at every point. For a non-linear or exponential function, the rate of change varies — it is different at each point.

The derivative is the mathematical tool that gives you the instantaneous rate of change at any specific point. This is the topic the QR-II past paper tested in Q5: "find the rate of change of P(t) = 1000e^(0.05t)".

For exponential P(t) = Aeʳᵗ:
Rate of change = dP/dt = rAeʳᵗ = r · P(t)

In plain English: the rate of change of an exponential function
is proportional to the current value.
The bigger the population, the faster it grows.

Basic derivative rules needed:
d/dt (Aeʳᵗ) = rAeʳᵗ
d/dt (aᵗ) = aᵗ · ln(a)
d/dt (tⁿ) = ntⁿ⁻¹ (power rule)
The QR-II Past Paper Q5 — Directly

P(t) = 1000e^(0.05t). "Find the rate of change at any time t" means find dP/dt. Apply the rule: d/dt(Aeʳᵗ) = rAeʳᵗ. Here r=0.05, A=1000. So dP/dt = 0.05 × 1000 × e^(0.05t) = 50e^(0.05t). "Rate of change after 10 years" means evaluate at t=10: dP/dt = 50e^(0.5) ≈ 50 × 1.6487 ≈ 82.4 individuals per year.

Mechanics & Notation
Choosing the right rule — identify the function's shape first:
Variable in the exponent (Aeʳᵗ, aᵗ) → use the exponential derivative rules
Variable in the base, raised to a fixed power (tⁿ) → use the power rule: d/dt(tⁿ) = ntⁿ⁻¹
Sum of terms → differentiate each term separately, then add the results

Procedure for evaluating a derivative at a specific point: first find the general derivative formula (a function of t), then substitute the specific t-value in afterward — never substitute first and differentiate a constant, which gives zero and loses all the information the question is asking for.

Worked Example
Applying the Power Rule — Not Every Rate of Change Is Exponential

A firm's revenue (in thousands of rupees) is modelled as R(t) = 5t² + 30t, where t is months since launch. Find the rate of change of revenue, and find it specifically at t=4 months.

R(t) = 5t² + 30t — sum of two power terms, not exponential
dR/dt = 5(2t) + 30(1) = 10t + 30 — power rule applied to each term separately

At t=4: dR/dt = 10(4) + 30 = 70

Revenue is growing at Rs. 70,000 per month at the 4-month mark. Notice this rate itself changes with t (10t + 30 is not constant) — even though this isn't an exponential model, the rate of change still varies over time, because the underlying function is non-linear. Constant rate of change is a property of linear functions specifically, not the absence of exponential growth.

T3 · A A lawyer argues in a damages case: "The rate of harm is increasing — therefore damages should be calculated using the current rate of harm, not the rate at the time of injury." The defendant responds: "The rate of harm was lower at the time of injury, so that is the correct baseline." Assuming harm follows H(t) = 200e^(0.08t), calculate the rate of harm at t=0 (time of injury) and t=5 (current time). Evaluate both legal arguments using the mathematics.
H(t) = 200e^(0.08t)
dH/dt = 0.08 × 200 × e^(0.08t) = 16e^(0.08t)

At t=0 (time of injury): dH/dt = 16e⁰ = 16 units/year
At t=5 (current): dH/dt = 16e^(0.4) = 16 × 1.4918 ≈ 23.9 units/year

Evaluation of the plaintiff's argument: Mathematically correct that the rate is increasing — from 16 to ~24 units/year over 5 years. Using the current (higher) rate to calculate ongoing damages captures the actual current impact. This is relevant to prospective damages: future harm will continue to increase.

Evaluation of the defendant's argument: Also has mathematical basis — at the moment of injury, the rate was 16 units/year, not 24. For retrospective damages (compensating harm already suffered), the rate at the time of each harm event is the correct measure. A single current-rate snapshot understates early harm and overstates early damages.

The correct resolution: Neither argument alone is complete. Retrospective damages should integrate the rate over time: total harm from t=0 to t=5 = ∫₀⁵ 16e^(0.08t)dt = [200e^(0.08t)]₀⁵ = 200e^(0.4) − 200 ≈ 297.4 − 200 = 97.4 units total. Prospective damages should use the current and projected rate. The dispute is actually about the time frame of damages being calculated, not which rate is correct — both parties are right about their respective time frames.

LEC 18
Integrated Modeling — Full Analysis
The Full Translation Checklist

This is the master checklist for any Part 3 word problem. Read the problem once, then work through these four questions before writing a single equation:

Step 1 — What type of change?
Fixed amount added each period → Linear (y = mt + c)
Fixed percentage each period → Exponential (y = a·bᵗ)
Two unknowns, two constraints → System of linear equations
Optimise subject to limits → Linear programming

Step 2 — Define variables explicitly.
"Let x = ..." before any equation. Always.

Step 3 — Write the model.
Equation(s), objective function, constraints — depending on type.

Step 4 — Answer the specific question asked.
Not "solve the model." Answer the question. Evaluate at the right point,
find the right intersection, interpret the result in context.
Worked Example — Step 1 in Isolation
Recognising the Model Before Computing Anything

Step 1 of the checklist — identifying the model type — is where most marks are actually lost, not in the algebra afterward. Four short scenarios, classified on sight, no computation:

(a) "A licensing fee rises by Rs. 2,000 every year." → Linear. Fixed amount, not a percentage.

(b) "Two firms' combined market share is 100%, and Firm A's share is three times Firm B's." → System of linear equations. Two unknowns (the two shares), two stated constraints.

(c) "A fine doubles for each repeated violation." → Exponential. "Doubles" is a multiplicative (percentage-equivalent) change, not a fixed addition.

(d) "A firm wants to maximise profit, subject to a labour cap and a materials budget." → Linear programming. An objective to optimise, plus multiple inequality constraints.

None of these required writing a single equation to classify correctly — the classification comes entirely from recognising the verbal signal (fixed amount, two constraints on two unknowns, multiplicative change, optimisation subject to limits) before any symbol is written down. Get this step right and the remaining three steps are mechanical; get it wrong and no amount of correct algebra recovers the right answer.

T2 · A A car travels 200 km partly at 40 km/h and partly at 60 km/h. The total time taken is 4 hours. Find the distance travelled at each speed. (This is the QR-I past paper Q2-ii.)

Model type: System of linear equations. Two unknowns, two constraints.

Let d₁ = distance at 40 km/h · d₂ = distance at 60 km/h

Equation 1 (total distance): d₁ + d₂ = 200
Equation 2 (total time): d₁/40 + d₂/60 = 4

From Eq.1: d₁ = 200 - d₂
Sub into Eq.2: (200-d₂)/40 + d₂/60 = 4

Multiply through by 120:
3(200-d₂) + 2d₂ = 480
600 - 3d₂ + 2d₂ = 480
-d₂ = -120 → d₂ = 120 km
d₁ = 200 - 120 = 80 km

Check: 80/40 + 120/60 = 2 + 2 = 4 hours ✓

T3 · A A regulatory agency monitors two factories. Factory A's emissions follow E_A(t) = 80 - 4t (linear reduction from a cleaning programme). Factory B's emissions follow E_B(t) = 60·(0.85)ᵗ (exponential reduction). A limit of 20 units is the legal threshold. Find when each factory falls below the threshold. At t=8, which factory has lower emissions? A lawyer argues: "Factory B is clearly performing better — it started lower and is reducing faster." Evaluate this argument using the mathematics.
Factory A below 20: 80 - 4t = 20 → 4t = 60 → t = 15 years

Factory B below 20: 60(0.85)ᵗ = 20
(0.85)ᵗ = 1/3
t·ln(0.85) = ln(1/3)
t = ln(0.333)/ln(0.85) = -1.0986/-0.1625 ≈ 6.76 years

At t=8:
E_A(8) = 80-32 = 48 units (still above threshold)
E_B(8) = 60(0.85)⁸ = 60(0.2725) ≈ 16.4 units (below threshold ✓)

Evaluating the lawyer's argument: The lawyer's claim has two parts — "started lower" and "reducing faster." Let's test each.

"Started lower": At t=0, E_A=80, E_B=60. True — Factory B started lower.

"Reducing faster": Factory A reduces by 4 units/year (constant linear rate). Factory B's rate of reduction: dE_B/dt = 60·ln(0.85)·(0.85)ᵗ. At t=0: 60·(-0.1625)·1 ≈ -9.75 units/year. Factory B initially reduces faster. But by t=10: dE_B/dt = 60·(-0.1625)·(0.85)^10 ≈ -60·0.1625·0.1969 ≈ -1.92 units/year — slower than Factory A's constant 4 units/year. So "reducing faster" is only true early on.

Overall verdict: The lawyer is correct that Factory B falls below the legal threshold first (~6.76 years vs. 15 years for A). In regulatory terms, Factory B achieves compliance sooner. However, the argument that it is "clearly performing better" based on initial conditions and early rate is an oversimplification — the comparison depends entirely on the time frame and the specific metric (compliance date, total emissions over a period, current level).
