Bivariate Data & Scatter Plots
MID-TERM
Bivariate Data & Scatter Plots
Language First

Bivariate data is just two measurements taken on the same set of things, paired up. "Bi" = two, "variate" = variable. Every single case in your dataset gives you a pair of numbers — not one number, two, that travel together.

A scatter plot is the picture of that pairing: one variable goes on the horizontal axis, the other on the vertical, and every pair becomes a single dot. The question a scatter plot answers in one glance is: "as one thing changes, does the other thing tend to change with it, and in which direction?"

Three things to read off a scatter plot, in plain English, before any formula touches it:

Direction: do the dots drift upward (positive) or downward (negative) as you move left to right?
Strength: do the dots hug a tight line, or are they scattered loosely around a vague trend?
Form: is the trend roughly a straight line, or does it curve?
The Translation Trap

"Association" and "causation" are not the same word, even when a word problem makes them sound interchangeable. A scatter plot can show ice-cream sales and drowning incidents both rising together through summer — strong positive association, zero causal link between the two. The hidden third factor (heat) drives both. PU-style questions will describe a strong relationship and ask you to evaluate a causal claim built on it; the correct answer is almost always "association does not establish causation" unless the question explicitly gives you a controlled experimental design.

Mechanics & Notation
Convention: the variable you suspect explains or predicts goes on x (explanatory/independent). The variable you're trying to predict or explain goes on y (response/dependent).

Positive association: low x pairs with low y, high x pairs with high y — dots trend up-right.
Negative association: low x pairs with high y, high x pairs with low y — dots trend down-right.
No association: dots form a shapeless cloud — knowing x tells you nothing about y.
Outlier: a point that sits well away from the overall pattern formed by the rest of the data.

Strength is a separate judgment from direction. A relationship can be strongly negative (tight downward line) or weakly positive (vague upward drift with lots of scatter) — direction and strength answer two different questions, and a question that asks for one is not answered by giving the other.

Worked Example
Reading a Scatter Plot — Hours of Revision vs. Errors Made
Hours Revised (X)12345678
Errors Made (Y)181613129753

Direction: as X rises, Y consistently falls — negative association.

Strength: the drop is steady and near-uniform at every step (18→16→13→12→9→7→5→3) — the points sit close to a single descending line, so this is a strong negative association, not just "some" negative association.

Form: the decrease is roughly constant per hour — this looks linear, not curved.

Plain-English conclusion before any number-crunching: more revision is strongly and consistently associated with fewer errors. Whether revision causes fewer errors, or whether more disciplined students simply both revise more and err less, is a separate question this scatter plot alone cannot answer.

Practice Questions
T2 · AA regulator plots "number of factory inspections per year" (X) against "reported safety violations" (Y) for 40 factories and finds a strong positive association. A factory owner argues: "This proves inspections cause violations — stop inspecting us." Identify the flaw and a more plausible explanation.

Flaw: the owner reads association as causation, and reverses the plausible causal direction without justification.

More plausible explanation: factories with more inspections find more violations because they're being looked at more carefully — not because inspecting causes the violations to occur. The violations were likely already there; inspection frequency determines detection rate, not occurrence rate.

Association vs. causation · reversed-causation fallacy · detection bias as an alternative explanation
T2 · BA scatter plot of "years of litigation experience" (X) against "case duration in months" (Y) for 50 lawyers shows points scattered with no visible trend in any direction. What does this tell a client choosing a lawyer, and what does it not tell them?

What it tells the client: in this dataset, years of experience shows no association with how long a case takes — knowing a lawyer's experience gives no predictive information about case duration.

What it does not tell the client: it doesn't mean experience is irrelevant to outcome quality, settlement value, or cost — only that it's unrelated to duration specifically, in this sample. No association on one variable pair says nothing about other variable pairs not plotted.

No-association interpretation · scope limitation of a single scatter plot · resisting overgeneralisation
T3 · AA study plots "monthly ice-cream sales" against "monthly drowning incidents" across a coastal town over 24 months and finds a strong positive association. A defendant in a wrongful-death suit involving a swimming pool argues this proves ice-cream consumption increases drowning risk, and seeks to introduce ice-cream sales data as causal evidence. As opposing counsel, construct the strongest rebuttal using the bivariate-analysis concepts above.

Core rebuttal: the association, however strong, is explained by a lurking third variable — ambient temperature/season. Hot months drive both more ice-cream purchases and more swimming (and therefore more drowning exposure). Neither variable causes the other.

Constructing the argument formally: demand the defendant show (a) a plausible causal mechanism linking ice-cream consumption to drowning physiologically, and (b) that the association survives after controlling for season/temperature. Without both, the correlation is consistent with — and far better explained by — the common-cause structure.

Why this matters in a wrongful-death context specifically: courts require causal evidence to meet a higher bar than mere statistical association (often "more likely than not" causation, not just correlation). A strong r-value alone, without a control for confounders, should not be admitted as proof of causal mechanism, and counsel should object to its introduction on exactly that basis.

Classic lurking-variable scenario applied to evidentiary standards · distinguishing statistical association from legal causation · constructing an evidentiary objection from a stats concept

Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · LSAT Connection

LSAT Logical Reasoning has an entire recurring question family built on exactly this concept: a stimulus presents a correlation, the conclusion claims causation, and the correct answer either identifies the missing causal mechanism or proposes the lurking variable. Recognising this pattern instantly — "correlation stated, causation concluded, where's the third factor" — is one of the highest-yield pattern recognitions on the exam.

In practice, this is also the single most common form of statistical evidence misuse in litigation involving epidemiology, environmental harm, and product liability — expert reports frequently present a strong association as if it settles causation, and a lawyer who can name the lurking-variable problem on sight has a real cross-examination advantage.

Pearson Correlation & Simple Linear Regression
MID-TERM
Pearson Correlation & Simple Linear Regression
Language First

A scatter plot shows you direction and strength by eye. Pearson's correlation coefficient (r) is the same judgment, turned into one precise number between -1 and +1, so two people don't have to argue about whether a relationship "looks fairly strong" — they can just compare numbers.

Regression answers a different, more ambitious question: not just "are these related," but "if I'm given a new X value I haven't seen before, what's my best single-number prediction for Y?" Regression fits the one straight line that sits closest to all the points at once, then lets you read predictions off that line.

r close to +1: strong positive — points hug an upward line
r close to -1: strong negative — points hug a downward line
r close to 0: little to no linear relationship
r is unitless — it doesn't matter if X is in rupees and Y is in kilometres, r is always between -1 and +1
The Translation Trap

"r = 0.3" sounds unimpressive but is not automatically "weak" or "unimportant" — strength judgments depend on field and context (r = 0.3 might be a major finding in social science, trivial in physics). A PU question that simply states an r-value and asks you to "comment" wants direction + a defensible strength judgment + an explicit refusal to claim causation from r alone, not a single-word answer.

Mechanics & Notation
Pearson's r:
r = [n·Σxy − Σx·Σy] / √([n·Σx² − (Σx)²][n·Σy² − (Σy)²])

Regression line: ŷ = b₀ + b₁x
b₁ (slope) = [n·Σxy − Σx·Σy] / [n·Σx² − (Σx)²]
b₀ (intercept) = ȳ − b₁x̄

Coefficient of determination: r² — the proportion of variation in Y "explained" by X. r² = 0.80 means 80% of the spread in Y is accounted for by its linear relationship with X; the remaining 20% is unexplained by this model.

Notice b₁'s formula is the numerator of r's formula, divided by only the x-side of the denominator. They share the same bones — r tells you how tightly the points cluster around a line; b₁ tells you the steepness of that line. A dataset can have a very steep slope and weak r (lots of scatter around a steep trend) or a shallow slope and strong r (tight scatter around a nearly flat trend) — slope and correlation strength are not the same thing.

Worked Example
Full Computation — Advertising Spend vs. Units Sold
Spend Rs'000 (X)246810121416
Units Sold (Y)1420243235404250
n = 8  ·  Σx = 72  ·  Σy = 257  ·  Σxy = 2726  ·  Σx² = 816
x̄ = 9.0  ·  ȳ = 32.125

r = [8(2726) − (72)(257)] / √([8(816)−72²][8(9285)−257²])
r = 2824 / √(816 × 9759) ≈ 0.9934

b₁ = [8(2726) − (72)(257)] / [8(816) − 72²] = 2824 / 1248 ≈ 2.4583
b₀ = 32.125 − (2.4583)(9.0) ≈ 10.0

Regression equation: ŷ = 10.0 + 2.4583x

Interpreting r = 0.9934: extremely strong positive linear relationship — advertising spend and units sold move together almost in lockstep within this data range.

Interpreting b₁ = 2.4583: each additional Rs. 1,000 spent on advertising is associated with roughly 2.46 more units sold, on average, within the observed range.

Interpreting b₀ = 10.0: the model predicts 10 units sold at zero advertising spend — this is the y-intercept, not necessarily a reliable real-world prediction, since x = 0 may sit outside or at the very edge of the data actually observed.

Prediction at x = 18 (extrapolation): ŷ = 10.0 + 2.4583(18) ≈ 54.25 units. Flag this explicitly as extrapolation — 18 lies outside the observed X range (2 to 16), so the prediction assumes the same linear relationship continues beyond the data, which is an assumption, not a guarantee.

r² = 0.9868: about 98.7% of the variation in units sold is explained by advertising spend in this model — only about 1.3% is left unexplained.

Practice Questions
T2 · AAn expert report states: "r = 0.85 between years since manufacture and product defect rate, therefore age causes defects." A lawyer challenges only the causal claim, not the r-value itself. Is this the right challenge to bring, and what should it say?

Yes, it's the right challenge. r = 0.85 is a strong positive correlation and the computation itself isn't in dispute — the leap from "strongly correlated" to "causes" is the actual weak point.

What it should say: r measures linear association only; it cannot by itself distinguish "age causes defects" from "age and defects share a common cause" (e.g., materials degrading with storage conditions) or even reverse causation in unusual cases. The expert needs to show a mechanism (e.g., material fatigue over time) and rule out confounders, not just cite r.

r as association, not proof of mechanism · structuring a precise expert-report challenge
T2 · BA regression model predicts monthly legal-aid case intake from local unemployment rate, fitted on data from months where unemployment ranged 4%–9%. A policymaker wants a prediction at 18% unemployment. What should you tell them about using this model, using the regression concepts above?

Flag it as extrapolation. 18% lies far outside the observed range (4%–9%) the line was fitted on. The model assumes the same linear relationship holds throughout, but there's no data evidence that relationship stays linear — or even stays positive — at unemployment levels double the highest value observed.

What to tell the policymaker: the prediction can be computed mathematically, but it carries no statistical guarantee; real-world relationships often change shape (saturate, accelerate, or reverse) outside the studied range, so the number should be presented as a rough extrapolation, not a reliable forecast.

Extrapolation risk · the boundary between interpolation and extrapolation · communicating model limitations to a non-technical decision-maker
T3 · AIn a wage-discrimination case, plaintiff's expert reports r = 0.40 between gender and salary (coded numerically) across 200 employees, claiming this proves discrimination. Defendant's expert reports a multiple regression controlling for experience, role, and education showing the gender effect drops to near zero once these are included. Reconcile the two reports using the concepts in this topic, and explain which is more probative and why.

Reconciliation: both reports can be numerically correct and not actually contradict each other. r = 0.40 (simple correlation, two variables only) measures the raw association between gender and salary, ignoring everything else that might differ systematically between genders in this workforce — e.g., if one gender group happens to have less average experience, that alone could produce a moderate r without any direct gender effect on pay.

The defendant's regression is more probative because it isolates the gender effect after holding the confounding variables (experience, role, education) constant — this is precisely the lurking-variable problem from the bivariate topic, addressed properly this time by controlling for the suspected confounders rather than ignoring them.

Important caveat for the plaintiff to raise in response: the defendant's model is only as good as the confounders it controls for — if "role" itself reflects historic discriminatory assignment (women systematically placed in lower-paid roles), controlling for role could mask rather than explain away the discrimination. The statistical question ("does the gender effect survive controls") and the legal question ("were the controls themselves products of discrimination") are different, and a court needs both addressed, not just the regression output taken at face value.

Simple correlation vs. multiple regression with controls · the lurking-variable problem applied to employment discrimination law · recognising when "controlling for X" can hide rather than explain a causal pathway — the kind of two-layer analytical move PU finals reward at Tier 3

Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Litigation Connection

Regression analysis underlies a huge share of expert testimony in employment discrimination, antitrust, securities fraud, and damages calculation cases — the "but-for" world in damages litigation is frequently built from a regression model's predicted value. A lawyer who understands what r² actually measures (proportion of variance explained, not "percent correct") and the interpolation/extrapolation boundary can ask the single most damaging cross-examination question to a poorly-supported regression: "how far outside your data's range are you asking this court to extrapolate?"

On the LSAT, the multiple-regression-with-controls scenario above is a direct preview of "alternative explanation" Logical Reasoning questions — the correct answer choice frequently identifies exactly this kind of confound that the stimulus's correlation hasn't ruled out.
