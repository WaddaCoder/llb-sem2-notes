Cluster 1 — Elementary Rules · Power & Addition/Subtraction
RULE 1
The Power Rule
Language First

When you have a variable raised to a fixed number — like x³ or t² or x⁰·⁵ — the power rule tells you the derivative in one step: bring the exponent down as a multiplier, then reduce the exponent by one. That's it. Every polynomial you will ever differentiate uses this rule, and it works for any real-number exponent — positive, negative, fraction, decimal.

The power rule is the most frequently used rule in calculus. Mastering it completely means every polynomial derivative becomes instant.

Where It Applies — and Where It Doesn't

Power rule applies when the variable is in the base and the exponent is a fixed constant: x³, t⁻², x^(½). It does NOT apply when the variable is in the exponent: 3ˣ, eˣ, e^(0.05t) — those need the exponential rule (Rule 5). This base-vs-exponent check is the first translation step every derivatives problem demands.

Mechanics & Notation
Power Rule: d/dx(xⁿ) = n·xⁿ⁻¹

Special cases worth memorising:
d/dx(x¹) = 1            (n=1: bring down 1, reduce to x⁰=1)
d/dx(c) = 0            (constant: n=0, result is 0·x⁻¹ = 0)
d/dx(x½) = ½x⁻½ = 1/(2√x) (square root as fractional power)
d/dx(1/x) = d/dx(x⁻¹) = -x⁻² = -1/x²

Procedure:
Step 1 — Rewrite: convert roots and fractions to xⁿ form
Step 2 — Multiply: bring the exponent down in front
Step 3 — Reduce: subtract 1 from the exponent
Step 4 — Simplify: clean up negative or fractional exponents if needed
Worked Example
Three Cases — Positive, Fractional, Negative Exponents

(a) f(x) = x⁵

f'(x) = 5x⁴   (bring down 5, reduce 5→4)

(b) f(x) = √x = x^(½)

f'(x) = ½x^(½−1) = ½x^(−½) = 1/(2√x)

(c) f(x) = 1/x³ = x^(−3)

f'(x) = −3x^(−3−1) = −3x^(−4) = −3/x⁴

In all three, the procedure is identical — rewrite, bring down, reduce by 1. The only difference is the starting form of the exponent. Rewriting as xⁿ before applying the rule avoids the most common errors.

Practice Questions
T2 · AA court fee schedule is modelled as F(c) = 0.04c³, where c is the claim value in millions. Find the marginal court fee — the rate at which the fee increases per additional million in claim value — and evaluate it at c = 5.

F'(c) = 0.04 × 3c² = 0.12c²

At c = 5: F'(5) = 0.12 × 25 = 3.0 — the fee is increasing at Rs. 3 million per additional million in claim value at that level. The non-linear schedule means higher-value claims are penalised progressively: doubling the claim doesn't double the marginal fee, it quadruples it.

T2 · BA statutory damages formula awards D(n) = 500√n to a plaintiff, where n is the number of verified violations. At n = 100 violations, find the rate at which additional violations increase the award.

D(n) = 500n^(½)

D'(n) = 500 × ½ × n^(−½) = 250/√n

At n = 100: D'(100) = 250/√100 = 250/10 = 25 — each additional violation at the 100-violation level adds Rs. 25 to the award. Notice this rate decreases as n grows (250/√n gets smaller): the scheme is deliberately degressive — the marginal deterrence of one more violation weakens as the violation count rises, which is a real policy criticism of square-root damage formulas.

T3 · AAn environmental regulator models the residual pollution concentration after t years of a cleanup programme as C(t) = 80/t² (in parts per million). (a) Find the rate of change of concentration at t = 4 years. (b) The legal safe threshold is 5 ppm — at what year does C(t) reach this threshold? (c) Is the cleanup rate accelerating or decelerating as t increases, and what does this imply for the cost of the final phase of cleanup?

(a) C(t) = 80t⁻²

C'(t) = −160t⁻³ = −160/t³

At t = 4: C'(4) = −160/64 = −2.5 ppm/year — concentration is falling at 2.5 ppm per year at year 4. The negative sign confirms improvement.

(b) 80/t² = 5 → t² = 16 → t = 4 years. The threshold is reached at exactly year 4.

(c) C'(t) = −160/t³ — the magnitude |C'(t)| = 160/t³ is itself decreasing over time (t³ grows). This means the rate of improvement slows down as cleanup progresses — the early years produce rapid concentration drops, but removing the final ppm takes disproportionately longer. Legally and financially, this is the "last mile" problem: the regulator will incur the majority of cost in the final phase, not the initial phase, because the mathematical rate of return on cleanup effort is highest at the start and diminishes to near zero as concentrations approach background levels. This asymmetry is often exploited in consent decree negotiations — the defendant firm pushes for performance-based milestones rather than time-based ones, knowing that later milestones are mathematically harder to hit.

Power rule with negative exponent · evaluating derivative at a point · solving C(t)=threshold for t · second-order interpretation (is the rate itself increasing or decreasing) · connecting diminishing-rate mathematics to litigation strategy
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Expert Witness Connection

Marginal analysis — which is simply the derivative evaluated at a specific point — is the core language of economics expert testimony in antitrust and damages litigation. A lawyer who can independently compute the marginal cost or marginal revenue figure the expert reports, from the underlying cost function, can verify it, challenge it, and cross-examine it on the stand rather than relying on the expert's number uncritically.

The "last mile" problem in environmental cleanup (T3 above) recurs across multiple bodies of law: remediation consent decrees, ADA architectural compliance timelines, and food safety corrective action plans all involve scenarios where the final percentage of compliance is exponentially more expensive than the first 90%, and the derivative's rate-of-change behaviour is the mathematical basis for every cost estimate the regulator and the regulated party will dispute.

RULE 2
The Addition & Subtraction Rule
Language First

When a function is built by adding or subtracting several pieces, its derivative is simply the sum (or difference) of each piece's individual derivative. Each term is handled separately, independently of the others. The rule is as simple as it sounds, and the only way to apply it wrong is to panic when a function has many terms and try to treat it as one complicated whole rather than a collection of simple pieces.

This rule also underlies why the power rule alone handles any polynomial — a polynomial is just several power-rule terms added together, so you differentiate each one and add the results.

Mechanics & Notation
Sum/Difference Rule:
d/dx[f(x) ± g(x)] = f'(x) ± g'(x)

Extended to any number of terms:
d/dx[u + v − w + z] = u' + v' − w' + z'

Constants ride through:
d/dx[5x³] = 5 · d/dx[x³] = 5 · 3x² = 15x²
(A constant multiplier stays attached to its term throughout)

The only common slip: forgetting to carry the constant multiplier all the way through when combining with the power rule. The constant never disappears — it just multiplies whatever the power rule produces.

Worked Example
Full Polynomial Differentiation

A law firm's total cost function is C(x) = 4x³ − 9x² + 12x − 7, where x is the number of cases handled per month. Find the marginal cost function C'(x) and the marginal cost at x = 3.

C'(x) = d/dx(4x³) − d/dx(9x²) + d/dx(12x) − d/dx(7)
      = 4(3x²) − 9(2x) + 12(1) − 0
      = 12x² − 18x + 12

At x = 3: C'(3) = 12(9) − 18(3) + 12 = 108 − 54 + 12 = 66

The marginal cost of handling the 3rd additional case is Rs. 66 (in whatever units C is expressed). Notice d/dx(7) = 0 — constants disappear on differentiation, because a fixed overhead cost doesn't change as you add one more case.

Practice Questions
T2 · AA regulatory fine schedule is modelled as F(v) = 3v⁴ − 8v² + 500, where v is the number of repeat violations. Find F'(v) and evaluate F'(2).

F'(v) = 12v³ − 16v

F'(2) = 12(8) − 16(2) = 96 − 32 = 64

At 2 repeat violations, each additional violation adds Rs. 64 to the fine — and because F' itself is increasing with v (12v³ dominates), the marginal fine escalates faster the more violations accumulate.

Sum/difference rule combined with power rule · evaluating at a point · interpreting escalating marginal penalties
T2 · BFind the derivative of f(x) = 6x⁵ − (4/x²) + 3√x − 11. Rewrite all terms as powers of x before differentiating.

Rewrite: f(x) = 6x⁵ − 4x⁻² + 3x^(½) − 11

f'(x) = 30x⁴ − (−8x⁻³) + (3/2)x^(−½) − 0

f'(x) = 30x⁴ + 8/x³ + 3/(2√x)

The rewrite step is what makes this tractable — never try to differentiate a fraction or square root in its original form without first expressing it as xⁿ.

Sum/difference rule · rewriting non-standard forms before differentiating · mixed positive, negative and fractional exponents
T3 · AA plaintiff's expert models the economic damage function over time as D(t) = 2t³ − 15t² + 36t (in thousands), where t is years since the breach. (a) Find D'(t). (b) Find the times at which the rate of damage is zero (set D'(t) = 0 and solve). (c) Interpret what "rate of damage = 0" means economically, and explain whether those are the times of maximum or minimum total accumulated damage.

(a) D'(t) = 6t² − 30t + 36

(b) 6t² − 30t + 36 = 0 → 6(t² − 5t + 6) = 0 → 6(t−2)(t−3) = 0 → t = 2 and t = 3

(c) D'(t) = 0 means the damage function is momentarily neither increasing nor decreasing at those points — these are the "turning points" of the damage curve. At t=2, damage accumulation pauses (a local maximum of the rate) and at t=3 it reaches a local minimum — meaning between years 2 and 3, the rate of new damage was actually declining. In a legal context, this matters significantly for the damages claim: if the breach's harm peaked at t=2 and began subsiding by t=3, a defendant might argue the plaintiff had an opportunity to mitigate after year 2, reducing their liability for post-peak damages. The plaintiff would argue those turning points were mathematically inevitable given the underlying contract structure, not a failure to mitigate.

Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Economic Damages Connection

Setting the derivative equal to zero to find turning points — the technique in T3 above — is exactly how economic experts identify the "peak damage" date in lost-profits litigation. Courts often need to determine when the plaintiff's losses were largest, when they began to decline, and whether a reasonable plaintiff would have taken mitigation steps at the turning point. A lawyer who can identify these turning points independently from the damage model has a genuine technical advantage in either preparing or attacking such testimony.

The "constants drop out" property of differentiation is also directly relevant to how fixed costs are treated in marginal analysis: in any damages or pricing model, fixed overheads are mathematically irrelevant to marginal rates, which is why economic experts report marginal (derivative-based) figures rather than total cost figures in incremental-harm cases.

Cluster 2 — Combination Rules · Product, Quotient & Chain
RULE 3
The Product Rule
Language First

When two functions are multiplied together, the derivative is not simply the product of their individual derivatives — this is the single most common misconception in introductory calculus and it is wrong every time. Instead: "differentiate the first, keep the second — then keep the first, differentiate the second" and add the two results.

The Wrong Version to Watch For

h'(x) ≠ u'(x)·v'(x). If your answer is the product of two derivatives, you've applied the wrong rule. The correct answer is always a sum of two terms.

Mechanics & Notation
Product Rule: if h(x) = u(x)·v(x), then
h'(x) = u'(x)·v(x) + u(x)·v'(x)

Procedure: Label u and v → find u' and v' separately → assemble u'v + uv' → simplify
Worked Example
Product Rule — h(x) = x³·eˣ
u = x³, v = eˣ → u' = 3x², v' = eˣ
h'(x) = 3x²·eˣ + x³·eˣ = eˣ(3x² + x³) = x²eˣ(3 + x)

Notice v' = eˣ because the derivative of eˣ is itself. The factoring step is optional but clean — the un-factored form is fully correct on an exam.

Practice Questions
T2 · ADifferentiate f(x) = x² · ln x. Show all product-rule steps.

u = x², v = ln x → u' = 2x, v' = 1/x

f'(x) = 2x·ln x + x²·(1/x) = 2x ln x + x = x(2 ln x + 1)

Product rule combining power and natural log derivatives
T2 · BA compensation model gives settlement value as S(t) = (2t + 3)(t² − 1). Find S'(t) and evaluate at t = 2.

u = 2t+3, v = t²−1 → u' = 2, v' = 2t

S'(t) = 2(t²−1) + (2t+3)(2t) = 2t²−2+4t²+6t = 6t²+6t−2

S'(2) = 24+12−2 = 34

Product rule with two polynomial factors · simplifying and evaluating
T3 · AA patent royalty model gives revenue as R(q) = q·(100 − 3q). (a) Find R'(q). (b) Find the revenue-maximising quantity by setting R'(q) = 0. (c) A defendant argues their unlicensed production of 10 thousand units caused "minimal lost revenue" because "the royalty rate would have been small at that volume." Using your derivative, evaluate this claim mathematically.

(a) u=q, v=100−3q → u'=1, v'=−3 → R'(q) = (100−3q) + q(−3) = 100−6q

(b) 100−6q=0 → q = 16.67 thousand units

(c) R'(10) = 100−60 = 40. At q=10, each additional thousand units generates 40 units of marginal royalty revenue — substantial, not minimal. Since q=10 is below the revenue-maximising point of 16.67, the defendant operated in the region of maximum marginal value. The "minimal lost revenue" argument would only be defensible if production exceeded 16.67, where marginal revenue turns negative. The defendant's claim is mathematically backwards.

RULE 4
The Quotient Rule
Language First

When one function is divided by another, use the quotient rule. The subtraction order in the numerator matters — reverse it and every sign is wrong. Memory hook: "low d-high minus high d-low, square the bottom and away we go."

Mechanics & Notation
Quotient Rule: if h(x) = u(x)/v(x), then
h'(x) = [u'(x)·v(x) − u(x)·v'(x)] / [v(x)]²

Procedure: Label u (high) and v (low) → find u', v' → assemble [u'v − uv'] → divide by v²
Worked Example
Quotient Rule — h(x) = (x²+1)/(x−3)
u = x²+1, v = x−3 → u' = 2x, v' = 1
h'(x) = [2x(x−3) − (x²+1)(1)] / (x−3)²
= [2x²−6x−x²−1] / (x−3)² = (x²−6x−1) / (x−3)²

The numerator is purely algebraic simplification after the rule is applied — expand, collect like terms, done.

Practice Questions
T2 · ADifferentiate f(x) = eˣ / x². Show all quotient-rule steps.

u=eˣ, v=x² → u'=eˣ, v'=2x

f'(x) = [eˣ·x² − eˣ·2x]/x⁴ = eˣ(x²−2x)/x⁴ = eˣ(x−2)/x³

Quotient rule combining exponential and power · factoring eˣ from the numerator
T2 · BA firm's average cost is AC(q) = (q² + 4q + 12)/q. Find AC'(q) using the quotient rule, then verify by simplifying the fraction first.

Quotient rule: u=q²+4q+12, v=q → u'=2q+4, v'=1

AC'(q) = [(2q+4)q − (q²+4q+12)] / q² = (q²−12)/q² = 1 − 12/q²

Verify by simplifying first: AC(q) = q + 4 + 12/q → AC'(q) = 1 + 0 − 12/q² = 1 − 12/q² ✓

Both routes give the same answer. Recognising when simplification before differentiation is cleaner is a time-saving exam skill.

Quotient rule · recognising equivalent simpler approach · average cost differentiation
T3 · AAn injury risk function is R(s) = s²/(s+10), where s is vehicle speed. (a) Find R'(s). (b) Evaluate R'(30) and R'(60). (c) A defendant argues that since R(60) ≈ 2×R(30), "doubling speed only doubles risk." Using R'(s), construct the precise counter-argument about marginal risk escalation.

(a) u=s², v=s+10 → u'=2s, v'=1

R'(s) = [2s(s+10)−s²]/(s+10)² = (s²+20s)/(s+10)²

(b) R'(30) = (900+600)/1600 = 0.9375 · R'(60) = (3600+1200)/4900 ≈ 0.9796

(c) The defendant compares total levels — a function-value comparison, not a rate comparison. The derivative shows that at 60 km/h, risk is growing at 0.98 units per km/h, versus 0.94 at 30 km/h. The marginal rate is converging toward 1 at high speeds: each additional km/h adds nearly one full risk unit with no diminishing effect. The function is accelerating toward a linear regime at high speeds, making each additional km/h progressively more dangerous in marginal terms. The defendant's framing conceals this escalation entirely.

Quotient rule · evaluating R' at two points · level vs marginal rate distinction · constructing a technical counter-argument
Bar & LSAT Lens
NY Bar · Products Liability Connection

The level-vs-rate distinction in T3 is one of the most common forms of expert-report sleight of hand in litigation. A lawyer who can identify which comparison is being made — function value or derivative — and demand the other where warranted removes a powerful rhetorical tool from opposing counsel. Average cost and its derivative underpin predatory pricing analysis in antitrust: the minimum of AC(q) (found by setting AC'(q)=0) defines the efficient scale, a key benchmark courts use when evaluating pricing below cost.

RULE 5
The Chain Rule
Language First

The chain rule handles functions inside other functions. The signal: a standard function (power, exponential, log) applied not to a bare x but to a whole expression — like (3x²+1)⁵, e^(x²), ln(4x+7). Rule: differentiate the outside first, leaving the inside untouched — then multiply by the derivative of the inside.

Most Commonly Forgotten Step

Students differentiate the outer correctly but forget to multiply by the inner derivative. Always ask: "did I multiply by the derivative of what's inside?"

Mechanics & Notation
Chain Rule: if y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

Common forms:
d/dx[(g(x))ⁿ] = n(g(x))ⁿ⁻¹ · g'(x)
d/dx[e^(g(x))] = e^(g(x)) · g'(x)
d/dx[ln(g(x))] = g'(x)/g(x)

Procedure: identify outer f and inner g → differentiate outer (inner stays intact) → differentiate inner → multiply
Worked Example
Three Chain Rule Forms

(a) y = (3x²+5)⁴

Outer (·)⁴ → 4(·)³ · Inner g'=6x → dy/dx = 24x(3x²+5)³

(b) y = e^(5x³)

Outer e^(·) → e^(·) · Inner g'=15x² → dy/dx = 15x²e^(5x³)

(c) y = ln(x²+1)

Outer ln(·) → 1/(·) · Inner g'=2x → dy/dx = 2x/(x²+1)
Practice Questions
T2 · ADifferentiate f(x) = (x³−4x)⁶. Identify the outer and inner functions before applying.

Outer: (·)⁶ → 6(·)⁵. Inner: x³−4x → 3x²−4

f'(x) = 6(3x²−4)(x³−4x)⁵

Chain rule with polynomial inner · labelling outer and inner before applying
T2 · BA bacterial population under drug treatment: P(t) = 1000·e^(−0.3t²). Find P'(t) and interpret its sign.

Outer: e^(·) → itself. Inner: −0.3t² → −0.6t

P'(t) = 1000·e^(−0.3t²)·(−0.6t) = −600t·e^(−0.3t²)

Negative for all t>0 — population is declining: the drug is effective. The rate of decline itself scales with current population (the eˣ factor), so the drug's effect weakens as fewer bacteria remain.

Chain rule with exponential outer and non-linear inner · interpreting sign of P'
T3 · ALitigation costs: C(t) = ln(2t²+8t+1) in millions. (a) Find C'(t). (b) Evaluate C'(1) and C'(5). (c) A defendant's insurer claims costs are "growing at a manageable, decelerating rate." Is this mathematically defensible, and does the deceleration support the insurer's framing?

(a) Outer: ln(·) → 1/(·). Inner: 2t²+8t+1 → 4t+8

C'(t) = (4t+8)/(2t²+8t+1)

(b) C'(1) = 12/11 ≈ 1.09 million/month · C'(5) = 28/91 ≈ 0.31 million/month

(c) Deceleration is real — the quadratic denominator grows faster than the linear numerator, so C'(t) genuinely falls over time. However, "manageable" is misleading: at t=1, costs grew at over 1 million per month, and C'(0)=8, meaning the rate was highest at day one. The deceleration began immediately, not after some initial stabilisation — which actually argues for early settlement, not for the insurer's exposure being manageable going forward. The insurer's framing implies costs were once worse and are now better; the mathematics says they were always decelerating from a high peak, which is a settlement argument for the plaintiff, not comfort for the insurer.

Cluster 3 — Transcendental Rules · Exponential, ln & Log
RULE 6
The Exponential Rule
Language First

The exponential function eˣ has a unique and extraordinary property: its derivative is itself. It is the only function in mathematics that is unchanged by differentiation. This is not a coincidence — it is the defining property of e as a number, and the reason e appears in every natural growth and decay model.

For a general base a (not e), the derivative introduces a factor of ln a — the natural logarithm of the base — because any base can be rewritten in terms of e, and that conversion leaves a ln a factor behind.

Mechanics & Notation
Base e: d/dx(eˣ) = eˣ
Base e, scaled: d/dx(Aeʳˣ) = rAeʳˣ   (chain rule applies — r is the inner derivative)
General base a: d/dx(aˣ) = aˣ · ln a
General base a, scaled: d/dx(aʳˣ) = r · aʳˣ · ln a

Why ln a appears: aˣ = e^(x ln a), so d/dx(e^(x ln a)) = e^(x ln a) · ln a = aˣ · ln a
Vs the Power Rule — Critical Distinction

d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹ — variable in the base, constant in the exponent → Power Rule.
d/dx(aˣ) = aˣ ln a — constant in the base, variable in the exponent → Exponential Rule.
Getting these backwards is the single most tested point of confusion across all derivatives problems.

Worked Example
Base e and General Base — Both Forms

(a) f(x) = 500e^(0.07x) — continuous compound growth at 7%

f'(x) = 0.07 × 500 × e^(0.07x) = 35e^(0.07x)
At x=10: f'(10) = 35e^(0.7) ≈ 35 × 2.0138 ≈ 70.48 units/period

(b) g(x) = 3ˣ — base 3, general exponential

g'(x) = 3ˣ · ln 3 ≈ 3ˣ × 1.0986
At x=2: g'(2) = 9 × ln 3 ≈ 9.887

Compare: if you mistakenly applied the power rule to 3ˣ, you'd get x·3^(x−1), which is completely wrong — the power rule requires the base to be the variable. This mistake gives a structurally different (and incorrect) function shape.

R'(t) = 0.12 × 2000 × e^(0.12t) = 240e^(0.12t)

R'(5) = 240e^(0.6) ≈ 240 × 1.8221 ≈ Rs. 437.3 per year at t=5.

T3 · ATwo competing brands grow their market share as A(t) = 200e^(0.15t) and B(t) = 350·(1.1)ᵗ. (a) Find A'(t) and B'(t). (b) Compare the growth rates at t=0 and t=10. (c) A regulator investigating market concentration argues that Brand A "will eventually dominate and should be regulated preemptively." Using your derivatives and the growth functions, evaluate whether this argument is mathematically supportable at this stage.

(a) A'(t) = 0.15×200×e^(0.15t) = 30e^(0.15t)

B'(t) = 350·(1.1)ᵗ·ln(1.1) ≈ 350·(1.1)ᵗ·0.09531 ≈ 33.36·(1.1)ᵗ

(b) At t=0: A'(0)=30, B'(0)≈33.36 — B growing faster initially.
At t=10: A'(10)=30e^1.5≈134.5, B'(10)=33.36·(1.1)^10≈33.36×2.5937≈86.5 — A now growing faster.

(c) The crossover in growth rates (A overtakes B somewhere between t=0 and t=10) does confirm A's trajectory is steeper. However, at t=0 Brand B still has a larger base (350 vs 200) and a higher initial growth rate — dominance is not yet established. The regulator's argument requires showing not only that A's growth rate eventually exceeds B's, but that A's total market share will overtake B's and produce sustained concentration. That requires finding the level crossover (A(t)=B(t)), not just the rate crossover. Setting 200e^(0.15t)=350·(1.1)ᵗ and solving for t gives the relevant threshold — until then, "will eventually dominate" remains a projection, not a present competitive reality, and premature regulation would be legally challengeable as anticipatory overreach without that crossover date specified and supported.

RULE 7
The Natural Logarithm Rule
Language First

The natural logarithm ln(x) is the inverse of eˣ — they undo each other. Their derivatives mirror this inverse relationship: where eˣ differentiates to itself (exponential growth), ln(x) differentiates to 1/x (which declines toward zero) — the rate of a logarithm slows continuously, which is exactly what "logarithmic growth" means in plain English.

ln(x) appears in economics, information theory, entropy calculations, and growth models wherever the rate of change is proportional to the reciprocal of the current level. In legal and statistical contexts, it also appears in the log-likelihood functions that underlie many expert statistical models — knowing how ln differentiates is prerequisite to understanding how those models are fitted.

Mechanics & Notation
Natural log rule: d/dx(ln x) = 1/x   (x > 0)

With a constant multiple: d/dx(a·ln x) = a/x

Combined with chain rule (composite):
d/dx[ln(g(x))] = g'(x)/g(x)

Domain note: ln x is only defined for x > 0. For expressions like ln|x|, the derivative is still 1/x but the domain extends to x ≠ 0. In word problems, the domain is almost always positive by context.
Worked Example
Natural Log — Simple and Composite Forms

(a) f(x) = 3 ln x

f'(x) = 3 · (1/x) = 3/x

(b) f(x) = ln(x² + 3x)

Chain rule: outer ln(·) → 1/(·), inner g = x²+3x → g' = 2x+3
f'(x) = (2x+3)/(x²+3x) = (2x+3)/(x(x+3))

(c) f(x) = x²·ln x — requires product rule AND natural log rule

u = x², v = ln x → u' = 2x, v' = 1/x
f'(x) = 2x·ln x + x²·(1/x) = 2x ln x + x = x(2 ln x + 1)

T2 · BA utility function is U(x) = ln(3x² + 6x), representing marginal utility of x units of a good. Find U'(x) and evaluate the rate of change of utility at x = 2.

Chain rule: g = 3x²+6x → g' = 6x+6

U'(x) = (6x+6)/(3x²+6x) = 6(x+1)/(3x(x+2)) = 2(x+1)/(x(x+2))

U'(2) = 2(3)/(2×4) = 6/8 = 0.75 — utility is growing at 0.75 units per additional good at x=2, a diminishing rate consistent with the principle of diminishing marginal utility.

T3 · AAn expert report models environmental harm accumulation as H(t) = t·ln(t) + 50 (measured in damage units, t in years, t≥1). (a) Find H'(t). (b) Evaluate H'(1), H'(5), H'(10). (c) A defendant argues that since ln(t) grows slowly, total harm is "barely increasing" at t=10. Construct a precise rebuttal using H'(10) and the product structure of H'(t).

(a) Product rule on t·ln(t): u=t, v=ln t → u'=1, v'=1/t

H'(t) = 1·ln t + t·(1/t) = ln t + 1

(b) H'(1) = ln 1 + 1 = 0+1 = 1 · H'(5) = ln 5+1 ≈ 1.609+1 = 2.609 · H'(10) = ln 10+1 ≈ 2.303+1 = 3.303

(c) The defendant exploits the true but partial observation that ln(t) itself grows slowly. The full derivative is H'(t) = ln(t)+1 — the "+1" term means H'(t) never falls below 1 regardless of how slowly ln(t) grows, and the total rate at t=10 is 3.303 damage units per year — more than three times the initial rate. "Barely increasing" is mathematically false: harm accumulation is accelerating, not stabilising. The product structure of H(t) = t·ln(t) creates this acceleration precisely because both factors are growing simultaneously, even if each grows slowly in isolation. A correct characterisation is "harm accumulates at a continuously rising rate, exceeding 3 units/year by year 10."

NY Bar · Environmental & Tort Connection

The "+1 floor" in H'(t) = ln(t)+1 is an example of how product-rule structures create acceleration that each individual factor conceals — a recurring source of expert-report errors in slow-onset environmental tort cases, where defendants routinely argue that "logarithmic harm" means minimal harm. The derivative reveals that the product of two slowly-growing functions can still accelerate significantly over time.

Log-linear functions also appear in economic efficiency models (Cobb-Douglas production functions are differentiated using the log rule), and understanding ln'(x)=1/x is prerequisite to understanding diminishing marginal returns — a concept that recurs in antitrust economics, regulatory economics, and damages calculations.

RULE 8
The Logarithm Rule (Base a)
Language First

The log base-a rule is the generalisation of the natural log rule to any base. It introduces a 1/ln a factor in the denominator — the same ln a that appeared in the general exponential rule's numerator. This is not a coincidence: log base a and exponential base a are inverses of each other, and their derivative rules are mirrors.

In practice, log base 10 (written log₁₀ or simply log) appears most often in measurement scales — the Richter scale for earthquakes, the decibel scale for sound, and pH in chemistry are all base-10 logarithms. Log base 2 appears in information theory and data compression. For any of these, the derivative is the base-a rule.

Mechanics & Notation
Log base-a rule: d/dx(logₐ x) = 1/(x · ln a)

Most common case — base 10: d/dx(log₁₀ x) = 1/(x · ln 10) ≈ 1/(x · 2.3026)

Why the ln a denominator? logₐ x = ln x / ln a (change-of-base formula). Differentiating: d/dx(ln x / ln a) = (1/x) · (1/ln a) = 1/(x ln a). The ln a in the denominator comes from dividing by a constant.

Combined with chain rule: d/dx[logₐ(g(x))] = g'(x) / (g(x) · ln a)
Worked Example
Log Base-a and Base-10 Derivatives

(a) f(x) = log₁₀(x)

f'(x) = 1/(x · ln 10) ≈ 1/(2.3026x)

(b) f(x) = log₂(3x² + 1)

Chain rule: g = 3x²+1 → g' = 6x, base a=2, ln 2 ≈ 0.6931
f'(x) = 6x / ((3x²+1) · ln 2) = 6x / (0.6931(3x²+1))

Comparison — why ln is usually preferred: d/dx(ln x) = 1/x, clean. d/dx(log₁₀ x) = 1/(x ln 10), an extra constant in the denominator always. In modelling contexts, engineers and scientists use ln specifically to avoid this constant — it's not that ln is more important, it's that its derivative is simpler. When you see log₁₀ in a real-world problem, the fastest approach is often to convert via log₁₀(x) = ln(x)/ln(10) before differentiating.

T2 · BA sound intensity model uses S(I) = 10·log₁₀(I/I₀), where I is intensity and I₀ is a constant. Find dS/dI and interpret it — what does it mean that dS/dI decreases as I increases?

S(I) = 10·log₁₀(I) − 10·log₁₀(I₀). I₀ is constant so its log term differentiates to zero.

dS/dI = 10 · 1/(I·ln 10) = 10/(I·ln 10)

As I increases, dS/dI = 10/(I·ln 10) decreases: diminishing marginal sensitivity. Each additional unit of sound intensity produces progressively smaller increases in perceived loudness (decibels). This is why the decibel scale was designed logarithmically — human hearing itself has this diminishing-returns structure, so a linear scale would be useless for expressing the enormous range of audible intensities. In an environmental noise case, this derivative shows precisely why a defendant's claim of "only a doubling of intensity" understates the perceived harm — doubling I from a low baseline adds far more perceived loudness than doubling from a high baseline.

Log base-10 rule · change of base · interpreting diminishing dS/dI as perceptual diminishing returns · applying to an environmental nuisance argument
T3 · AAn information-theoretic damages model uses D(p) = −K·p·log₂(p), where p is the probability of a data breach occurring and K is a scaling constant. (a) Find D'(p) using the product rule and log base-2 rule. (b) Evaluate the sign of D'(p) for 0 < p < 1/e^(ln 2) and interpret. (c) A defendant argues that their negligence increased breach probability from 0.1 to 0.2 — "merely doubling a small probability." Using D'(p), construct the correct characterisation of the incremental harm.

(a) D(p) = −K·p·log₂(p). Product rule: u=p, v=log₂(p) → u'=1, v'=1/(p·ln 2)

D'(p) = −K[1·log₂(p) + p·(1/(p·ln 2))] = −K[log₂(p) + 1/ln 2]

Since 1/ln 2 = log₂(e): D'(p) = −K[log₂(p) + log₂(e)] = −K·log₂(ep)

(b) D'(p) = 0 when log₂(ep) = 0 → ep = 1 → p = 1/e ≈ 0.368. For p < 1/e: log₂(ep) < 0, so D'(p) = −K·(negative) = positive — D(p) is increasing. Harm rises as breach probability increases from 0 toward 1/e, which covers all practically relevant probability ranges (small breach probabilities are the ones that matter in most security contexts).

(c) D'(0.1) = −K·log₂(e×0.1) = −K·log₂(0.2718) ≈ −K×(−1.879) = 1.879K

D'(0.2) = −K·log₂(e×0.2) = −K·log₂(0.5436) ≈ −K×(−0.879) = 0.879K

The marginal damage rate at p=0.1 is 1.879K — more than double the marginal rate at p=0.2. This means the increase from 0.1 to 0.2 passes through the region of highest marginal sensitivity. "Merely doubling a small probability" is exactly the wrong framing: moving probability in the low-p region causes the steepest incremental harm of any equivalent probability increase, not the most negligible. The defendant's characterisation inverts the mathematical reality — small probability increases near p=0 carry the highest marginal damage, which is precisely why security standards impose strict controls on negligent increases in breach likelihood in that range.
