LEC 1
Introduction to Logic & Its Importance
Language First

Logic is the study of correct reasoning — the rules that separate valid arguments from invalid ones. It is not about whether a conclusion is true in the world. It is about whether the conclusion follows from the premises given.

A doctor diagnosing from symptoms, a judge evaluating evidence, a lawyer constructing an argument — all are applying logic, whether or not they label it that way. Logic makes the structure of that reasoning visible and testable.

Why it matters for law specifically: Every legal rule is a conditional statement. Every case involves premises (facts) and a conclusion (verdict or liability). A lawyer who cannot identify flawed reasoning cannot challenge it. Logic is the grammar of legal argument.

PU Exam Pattern

Punjab University tests logic understanding through application, not definition. Expect a scenario where you must identify whether an argument is logically sound — not recite what logic is. Always ask: do the premises actually support the conclusion, or are they just related to it?

Mechanics & Notation

Every argument, however it's dressed up in prose, reduces to the same skeleton: one or more premises offered as support, and a conclusion they're meant to establish.

Standard form:
P1: [premise]
P2: [premise]
∴ C: [conclusion]

Premise indicators: since, because, given that, as, for
Conclusion indicators: therefore, thus, hence, so, it follows that, consequently

Spotting these indicator words is the first mechanical skill in logic, and the one most word problems lean on to hide their structure — a sentence with no "therefore" in it can still be an argument; the conclusion just has to be located by meaning rather than by signal word.

Worked Example
Extracting Standard Form

"Since the contract was signed under duress, and contracts signed under duress are voidable, this contract is voidable."

P1: The contract was signed under duress.
P2: Contracts signed under duress are voidable.
∴ C: This contract is voidable.

Notice "since" flags the premises, and the conclusion is stated last but isn't marked with a conclusion-indicator word at all — it has to be identified as the claim the rest of the sentence is built to support, not located by a keyword.

T3 · A A regulator's press release states: "Inspections increased 40% this year. Compliance complaints fell 25%. Our enforcement program is working." Restate this as a formal argument in standard form, and then evaluate — using only the structure, not outside knowledge — whether the conclusion actually follows from the premises as given.

P1: Inspections increased 40% this year.
P2: Compliance complaints fell 25%.
∴ C: The enforcement program is working.

Does the conclusion follow? Not from these premises alone. The two premises are both true claims about what happened, but neither one, nor both together, logically establishes that the enforcement program caused the improvement — an unstated assumption (that the two trends are causally linked, and that no other factor explains the drop in complaints) is doing all the actual work. The argument as stated commits exactly the gap this entire subject is built to catch: premises that are merely related to the conclusion being treated as premises that establish it.

LEC 2
Inductive, Deductive & Abductive Reasoning
Language First

Deductive: General rule → specific conclusion. The conclusion is guaranteed if the premises are true. "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. Therefore Socrates is mortal." The conclusion cannot be false if both premises are true. This is certainty.

Inductive: Specific observations → general conclusion. The conclusion is probable, not guaranteed. "Every swan I have ever seen is white. Therefore all swans are white." One black swan in Australia destroyed this conclusion. This is probability, not certainty.

Abductive: Observation → best available explanation. "The grass is wet. The most likely explanation is that it rained." It could have been a sprinkler — but rain is the simplest hypothesis. This is the reasoning of diagnosis, investigation, and circumstantial evidence.

The Disguise to Watch For

PU will present an argument and ask which type of reasoning it uses — without labelling it. The tell: if the conclusion is guaranteed by the premises, it is deductive. If it is probable from accumulated evidence, it is inductive. If it is the best explanation for a single observation, it is abductive.

Worked Example
Classifying Three Arguments

(a) "Every contract requires offer, acceptance, and consideration. This agreement has all three. Therefore it is a valid contract." — Deductive. The conclusion is guaranteed if both premises are true; nothing about contract law is left to probability here.

(b) "The last six judgments from this bench have favoured the plaintiff in negligence claims. The seventh case is likely to favour the plaintiff too." — Inductive. A pattern from six specific cases is used to predict a seventh; the conclusion is probable, not certain — a sufficiently different seventh case could break the pattern.

(c) "The safe was found open, the cash missing, and only the manager had the combination. The manager is the most likely suspect." — Abductive. This is the simplest explanation for the observations, not the only logically possible one — someone else could have obtained the combination, but absent further evidence, the manager is the best available explanation.

Practice Questions
T2 · A A judge reasons: "In the past 20 cases of this type, courts awarded damages. Therefore damages should be awarded here." Identify the reasoning type and its weakness.

Reasoning type: Inductive. The judge moves from specific past cases to a general conclusion about this case.

Weakness: Inductive conclusions are never guaranteed. The 21st case may have a distinguishing fact that breaks the pattern. In law, this is why precedent requires careful case distinction — a prior pattern does not automatically govern a new case.

Inductive reasoning · identifying the gap between probability and certainty · legal application to precedent
T2 · B Person A's fingerprints are on the weapon. Person A was seen near the scene. The detective concludes Person A is the most likely suspect. What type of reasoning is this, and what would make it deductive instead?

Reasoning type: Abductive. The detective selects the simplest explanation for the observations — not the only possible explanation.

To make it deductive: A deductive argument would require a premise that guarantees the conclusion — for example, "Only the person whose fingerprints are on the weapon and who was near the scene committed the crime." Then the conclusion follows necessarily. But such a premise would rarely be true in reality, which is why criminal cases rely on the standard of proof (beyond reasonable doubt) rather than logical certainty.

Abductive vs. deductive distinction · the gap between circumstantial evidence and proof · standard of proof as a logical concept
T3 · A A lawyer argues: "Environmental regulations always reduce economic growth. This country introduced environmental regulations last year. Economic growth has declined. Therefore the regulations caused the decline." Identify every reasoning type used and every logical flaw present.

Reasoning types present: The first premise ("always reduce") is itself an inductive generalisation stated as if it were a universal rule. The argument then applies it deductively to this country. The link between regulations and the observed decline is abductive — it assumes the simplest explanation without ruling out alternatives.

Logical flaws:

1. The inductive premise is overstated as universal ("always"). One counterexample destroys it.

2. Correlation is mistaken for causation — growth declined, regulations were introduced, but no causal mechanism is established. A global recession, political instability, or currency devaluation could explain the decline equally well.

3. Post hoc ergo propter hoc fallacy — "after this, therefore because of this." Temporal sequence is not causal proof.

Bar relevance: Expert witnesses in economic regulation cases make exactly this argument. Cross-examination turns on identifying the causal claim and demanding evidence for it beyond temporal correlation.

Mixed reasoning types in a single argument · overgeneralisation · correlation vs. causation · post hoc fallacy · Tier 3 because it requires simultaneously identifying the structure and multiple flaws

NY Bar · LSAT Connection

IRAC is deductive reasoning in legal dress. The Rule is the general premise. Application checks whether the facts satisfy it. Conclusion follows necessarily if they do. Understanding this makes IRAC mechanical rather than mysterious.

Inductive reasoning underlies common law development — courts observe patterns across cases and extract general rules. Abductive reasoning is the engine of investigation and diagnosis. All three appear on the LSAT under different question labels.

LEC 3
Propositions, Arguments & Logical Connectives
Language First

A proposition is any statement that is either true or false — never both, never neither. "The court is in session" is a proposition. "Is the court in session?" is not — questions have no truth value.

An argument is a set of propositions where some (the premises) are offered as reasons to believe another (the conclusion). A valid argument is one where if the premises are true, the conclusion cannot be false.

Logical connectives join propositions to build compound statements:

¬p Negation — "not p" — flips the truth value
p ∧ q Conjunction — "p and q" — true only when BOTH are true
p ∨ q Disjunction — "p or q" — false only when BOTH are false
p → q Conditional — "if p then q" — false ONLY when p is true and q is false
p ↔ q Biconditional — "p if and only if q" — true when both have the SAME truth value
The Conditional Trap — Most Tested

p → q is false in exactly ONE situation: p is true and q is false. Students instinctively apply real-world logic and say "if p is false, the conditional is violated." Wrong. If p is false, the conditional makes no claim at all — it is vacuously true. PU exploits this constantly.

Worked Example
Translating Compound Statements

"The defendant is liable if and only if both negligence is proven and the harm was foreseeable."

Let p = negligence is proven, q = harm was foreseeable, r = defendant is liable.

r ↔ (p ∧ q)

Reading it back into English to check the translation: "r if and only if (p and q)" — liability holds exactly when both negligence and foreseeability hold together, and fails if either is missing. This matches the original sentence, confirming the translation is correct in both directions, which is the actual test of a correct symbolisation — not just going English-to-symbols, but symbols-to-English landing back on the same meaning.

Practice Questions
T2 · A A contract clause states: "If payment is received by Friday, the goods will be delivered Monday." Payment was not received by Friday. Was the clause violated?

No, the clause was not violated. The clause is a conditional p → q where p = "payment received by Friday" and q = "goods delivered Monday." When p is false (payment not received), the conditional is vacuously true regardless of what happens to q. The clause makes no promise about what happens when payment is not received — it only promises delivery when payment is made. Not delivering when payment was not received is entirely consistent with the clause.

T3 · A A statute reads: "A person is liable if and only if they caused harm AND acted negligently." Person A caused harm but did not act negligently. Person B acted negligently but caused no harm. Person C caused harm and acted negligently. Determine the liability of each person and explain what the biconditional adds beyond a simple conditional.

Let L = liable, H = caused harm, N = acted negligently. The statute: L ↔ (H ∧ N)

Person A: H = T, N = F → H ∧ N = F → L = F. Not liable.

Person B: H = F, N = T → H ∧ N = F → L = F. Not liable.

Person C: H = T, N = T → H ∧ N = T → L = T. Liable.

What the biconditional adds: A simple conditional (H ∧ N) → L would only say: if both conditions met, then liable. It would leave open whether a person could be liable some other way. The biconditional L ↔ (H ∧ N) closes that door entirely — liability exists if and only if both conditions are met. It is both a sufficient and necessary condition. This precision matters for statutory drafting: it prevents courts from finding liability through alternative routes not specified in the statute.

LEC 3
Truth Tables & Propositional Equivalences
Language First

Two compound statements are logically equivalent if they have identical truth values in every possible situation — every row of their truth tables matches.

The three equivalences you must know cold:

De Morgan 1: ¬(p ∧ q) ≡ ¬p ∨ ¬q
"Not (both p and q)" = "either not p, or not q"

De Morgan 2: ¬(p ∨ q) ≡ ¬p ∧ ¬q
"Not (p or q)" = "neither p nor q"

Contrapositive: p → q ≡ ¬q → ¬p
"If p then q" = "If not q, then not p"
Contrapositive vs. Converse — Critical Distinction

The contrapositive (¬q → ¬p) is logically equivalent to the original (p → q). The converse (q → p) is NOT equivalent — it is a completely different claim. Confusing these two is the single most exploited error on the LSAT and in legal argument analysis.

Mechanics & Notation

To prove two statements equivalent, build a truth table for each and compare column-by-column across all rows — equivalence means every row matches, with no exceptions.

Procedure:
1. List all variables, build all 2ⁿ truth-value combinations
2. Evaluate each statement's truth value row by row
3. Compare the final columns — identical in every row = equivalent (≡); differ in even one row = not equivalent

For two variables (p, q), that's 4 rows. For three variables, 8 rows. The number of rows is always 2ⁿ, where n is the number of distinct propositional variables — this is itself a fact PU has tested directly ("how many rows in the truth table for a statement with 4 variables?").

T3 · A A defence counsel argues: "The statute says if a person is negligent, they are liable. My client is not negligent. Therefore my client is not liable." The prosecution responds: "That reasoning is flawed." Who is right, and why? Identify the exact logical error if one exists.

The prosecution is right. The defence has committed the Fallacy of Denying the Antecedent.

Valid pattern (Modus Tollens): p → q, ¬q ∴ ¬p ✓
Invalid pattern (Defence): p → q, ¬p ∴ ¬q ✗

The statute says negligence is sufficient for liability — not that it is the only path to liability. The defendant might be liable through a different legal theory (strict liability, intentional tort, vicarious liability) even without negligence. The conditional p → q does not say ¬p → ¬q. That would be the inverse, which is not logically equivalent to the original.

What the defence needed to argue: That the statute uses "if and only if" (biconditional) — making negligence both necessary and sufficient for liability. If that were the case, ¬p would indeed give ¬q. But that is a statutory interpretation argument, not a logical deduction from the stated conditional.

T2 · DExplain the difference between the converse and the contrapositive of p→q. For the statement "If a contract is void, it has no legal effect," write both the converse and the contrapositive. Which, if either, must be true given the original? Which is the most dangerous mistake to make in legal practice?

Contrapositive of p→q: ¬q→¬p. Logically equivalent to the original — if the original is true, the contrapositive is necessarily true. No additional information needed.

Converse of p→q: q→p. Logically independent of the original — the original can be true while the converse is false, and vice versa. They make different claims.

Original: V→¬L (void → no legal effect).

Contrapositive: L→¬V (has legal effect → not void). Must be true. If a contract has legal effect, it cannot be void.

Converse: ¬L→V (no legal effect → void). Does NOT have to be true. A contract might have no legal effect for reasons other than voidness — it might be unenforceable, or its performance might be complete, or it might be voidable but not yet avoided. "No effect" does not automatically mean "void."

Most dangerous mistake: treating the converse as if it were the contrapositive — assuming "the contract has no legal effect, therefore it must be void" when in fact voidness is only one of several reasons a contract might lack legal effect. In practice, this error can cause lawyers to advise clients incorrectly about whether a contract needs to be formally voided rather than simply treated as unenforceable.

The contrapositive/converse confusion is, by a wide margin, the single most exploited structural error on the LSAT's Logical Reasoning and Logic Games sections — entire "Necessary Assumption" and "Flaw" question types are built around an answer choice that swaps a conditional for its converse and hopes you won't notice.

LEC 4
Logical Fallacies
Language First

A logical fallacy is an argument that appears valid but contains a flaw in its reasoning. The conclusion may even be true — but it does not follow from the premises in the way claimed. Fallacies are the tools of persuasion that logic exposes.

Ad Hominem: Attacking the person, not the argument.
"Person A is corrupt, so their evidence is false."

Straw Man: Misrepresenting the opponent's position to attack an easier target.

False Dichotomy: Presenting two options as if they are the only ones.
"Either you support this policy or you support crime."

Post Hoc: Assuming causation from temporal sequence.
"Crime rose after the law changed, so the law caused crime."

Circular Reasoning: The conclusion is hidden in the premises.
"This law is just because just laws are those passed by Parliament."

Denying the Antecedent: p→q, ¬p, ∴ ¬q (invalid — see Topic 4)

Affirming the Consequent: p→q, q, ∴ p (invalid — converse error)
PU Pattern — Name AND Explain

PU will not just ask you to name a fallacy. It will present an argument and ask you to identify the flaw AND explain why it is a flaw. Naming alone scores partial marks. The explanation is where full marks live.

Mechanics & Notation

Fallacies split into two structurally different categories, and naming which category you're in is itself half the answer on a PU question.

Formal fallacies: the structure itself is broken — affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent. These can be shown invalid with a truth table alone, with no need to know what p and q actually mean.

Informal fallacies: the structure is fine — ad hominem, straw man, post hoc, circular reasoning all have a valid-looking shape — but the content is flawed: an irrelevant premise, a misrepresented claim, an unjustified causal leap.

In formal fallacies, the logical plumbing itself is broken. In informal fallacies, the plumbing works fine, but what's flowing through it is the problem. That distinction tells you where to locate the flaw: in the symbolic structure, or in the real-world content the structure carries.

Worked Example
Diagnosing a Fallacy, Fully

"Counsel's argument on environmental liability can't be trusted — their firm has previously represented oil companies."

Step 1 — name it: Ad Hominem.

Step 2 — locate the category: Informal. The sentence has a perfectly normal argument shape ("X is unreliable because of Y"); nothing about its symbolic structure is broken.

Step 3 — explain the flaw (where full marks live): the firm's past clients are irrelevant to whether the legal argument itself is structurally sound. The objection attacks who is speaking instead of engaging with the statutory analysis presented — even a lawyer with a conflicted client history can construct a logically valid argument, and even a lawyer with a spotless history can construct a flawed one. Past representation says nothing about present argument quality.

Practice Questions
T2 · A A prosecutor says: "Defence counsel has previously been sanctioned for misconduct. Therefore his arguments in this case should be disregarded." Identify the fallacy and explain its effect on the argument.

Fallacy: Ad Hominem. The prosecutor attacks the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Even if defence counsel has been sanctioned, that fact has no logical bearing on whether the arguments in this case are sound. A flawed person can make a valid argument. A reputable person can make a fallacious one. The merit of an argument is independent of the character of its maker.

The error: It eliminates the middle ground entirely and misrepresents the opponent's actual position (Straw Man) by attributing a motive (wanting terrorism) that was never claimed.

Valid argument against the bill: "The bill's detention-without-trial provision violates Article X of the Constitution. Courts have consistently held that such provisions require judicial oversight. Therefore the bill as drafted is unconstitutional, irrespective of its anti-terrorism objectives."

T3 · A A judge's written opinion states: "This court finds the defendant's conduct unlawful because unlawful conduct is precisely what this court was established to identify and condemn." Identify all logical flaws, explain why the reasoning cannot support the judgment, and state what the judgment would require to be logically sound.

Primary fallacy: Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question). The conclusion (conduct is unlawful) is embedded in the premise (this court condemns unlawful conduct). The argument moves from "the court condemns this" to "this is unlawful" using a premise that assumes the very thing it is trying to prove.

Secondary issue: The premise about the court's purpose — even if true — only establishes jurisdiction, not the merits. A court established to identify unlawful conduct has the authority to make findings, but that authority does not itself constitute a finding.

Why this cannot support the judgment: A sound judicial finding requires (1) identifying the applicable legal rule, (2) finding the relevant facts, and (3) applying the rule to the facts to reach a conclusion. The opinion skips steps 1 and 2 entirely and attempts to derive the conclusion from institutional authority alone.

What sound reasoning would require: "The defendant's conduct satisfies the elements of [specific statutory provision / common law rule]. The court finds each element established on the evidence. Therefore the conduct is unlawful." This is deductive: rule (major premise) + facts (minor premise) → conclusion.

Circular reasoning · jurisdictional authority vs. substantive finding · the three-step structure of sound judicial reasoning · Tier 3 because it requires identifying the fallacy, explaining why it specifically fails in a judicial context, and reconstructing what valid reasoning would look like

Lec. 5–7 · Venn Diagrams, Predicates & Quantifiers
LEC 5
Venn Diagrams & Categorical Reasoning
Language First

A Venn diagram makes the relationship between sets visible. The four categorical relationships you need:

All A are B: Circle A is entirely inside Circle B
No A are B: Circles A and B do not overlap at all
Some A are B: Circles A and B partially overlap
Some A are not B: Circle A exists partly outside Circle B

The key use of Venn diagrams is testing argument validity. Draw the premises. If the conclusion is already visible in the diagram, the argument is valid. If the conclusion could be false given the diagram, the argument is invalid.

PU Exam Pattern — QR-II Past Paper Q1-xii

The past paper asked: "All apples are fruits. All fruits are healthy. Draw inferences using a Venn diagram." This is a two-premise syllogism. Draw the three circles — Apples inside Fruits, Fruits inside Healthy — and the inference (all apples are healthy) is immediately visible as a transitive relationship.

Mechanics & Notation
A ⊂ B: A is a subset of B — "All A are B" — every member of A is also in B
A ∩ B = ∅: A and B are disjoint — "No A are B" — zero overlap
A ∩ B ≠ ∅: A and B overlap — "Some A are B" — at least one shared member
Transitivity: if A ⊂ B and B ⊂ C, then A ⊂ C — chains of "all" statements combine

Validity test procedure: draw every premise as accurately as the diagram allows — if a premise uses "some," draw only a partial, ambiguous overlap, not a full circle relationship, since "some" never licenses you to draw more certainty than the premise actually states. Then check: is the conclusion forced by every way the diagram could legally be drawn, or only true in one possible drawing? If only one possible drawing makes it true, the argument is invalid — a valid conclusion must hold in every diagram consistent with the premises, not just the first one you happen to draw.

Worked Example
Testing a Three-Circle Syllogism

Premise 1: All witnesses are required to take an oath. Premise 2: Some witnesses are minors. Conclusion offered: Some minors are required to take an oath.

Drawing it: Witnesses ⊂ Required-to-Oath (P1, full containment). Minors overlaps Witnesses partially (P2, "some").

Checking the conclusion: the overlap region between Minors and Witnesses sits entirely inside Required-to-Oath, because all of Witnesses does. So that overlap region — which represents witnesses who are minors — is necessarily inside Required-to-Oath too.

Valid. Every way you could legally draw this diagram consistent with both premises forces some minors into the Required-to-Oath circle — there's no valid drawing that avoids it.

T3 · A Premise 1: No acquitted person is guilty. Premise 2: Some guilty persons are dangerous. Conclusion offered: Some dangerous persons are not acquitted. Is this valid? Use Venn diagram reasoning and explain the limits of what can be inferred from these premises.

From Premise 1: Acquitted and Guilty are disjoint — the circles do not overlap. From Premise 2: Guilty and Dangerous partially overlap — some guilty persons are in the Dangerous circle.

Since Guilty and Acquitted are entirely separate, anything in the Guilty circle (including the part that overlaps with Dangerous) is outside the Acquitted circle. Therefore the guilty-and-dangerous persons are not acquitted — making "some dangerous persons are not acquitted" valid.

Limits of inference: We cannot conclude that ALL dangerous persons are not acquitted — the Dangerous circle may extend into regions with no Guilty overlap, and some of those persons could theoretically be acquitted. We also cannot conclude anything about whether acquitted persons are dangerous — the Acquitted circle is entirely separate from Guilty but may or may not overlap with Dangerous independently.

Legal implication: This diagram captures the structural tension between legal innocence (acquittal) and factual guilt — they are logically separable categories, which is why the criminal standard of proof ("beyond reasonable doubt") acknowledges that acquittal does not prove actual innocence.

LEC 6
Predicates & Predicate Logic
Language First

A predicate is a property or relationship — something that can be true or false of an individual. "is honest" is a predicate. "is taller than" is a predicate. On their own, predicates are not propositions — they become propositions when applied to a specific subject.

P(x) means "x has property P." It is true for some values of x and false for others.

P(x) = "x is a licensed lawyer"
P(Person A) = true if Person A is licensed, false otherwise

R(x, y) = "x is the employer of y"
R(Company, Employee) = true or false depending on the specific pair

Predicate logic is more powerful than propositional logic because it can express who or what a claim applies to — not just whether a compound statement is true or false.

Mechanics & Notation
Unary predicate: P(x) — one subject, one property: "x is licensed"
Binary predicate: R(x, y) — a relationship between two subjects: "x employs y"
Compound predicates: built from connectives, e.g. H(x) ∧ ¬G(x) — "x was harmed and x did not consent"
Domain: the set of things x is allowed to range over — always state it, since a predicate's truth depends on which domain you're evaluating it against

The mechanical skill here is plugging a specific individual into a predicate and evaluating true/false, then carrying that result through whatever propositional logic (∧, →, etc.) wraps around it — exactly the same truth-table machinery from Topic 3 and 4, just applied to predicates that have first been evaluated for a named subject.

Worked Example
Evaluating a Binary Predicate

Let R(x, y) = "x is the employer of y." A workplace policy states: "If x is the employer of y, x must provide y with a written contract." Formally: ∀x∀y, R(x,y) → C(x,y), where C(x,y) = "x provides y a written contract."

Company Z employs Person D. Apply the rule: R(Z, D) = T (given). By modus ponens on the universal rule, C(Z, D) must be T — Company Z is required to provide Person D a written contract.

Notice the rule says nothing about Company Z's obligations to anyone it does not employ — R(Z, anyone-else) = F leaves the conditional vacuously true, with no obligation triggered, exactly the same "false antecedent" pattern from Topic 3's conditional, just now carrying two subjects instead of one proposition.

LEC 7
Quantifiers — Universal & Existential
Language First

Quantifiers tell you how much of a domain a predicate applies to.

∀x P(x) — Universal: P is true for EVERY x in the domain
"All lawyers are officers of the court"

∃x P(x) — Existential: P is true for AT LEAST ONE x in the domain
"Some lawyers are also politicians"

The domain matters enormously. ∀x P(x) over the domain "licensed lawyers in Pakistan" is a very different claim from ∀x P(x) over "all persons."

Mechanics & Notation
Proving ∀x P(x): requires showing P holds for every single element of the domain — one missing case disproves it
Proving ∃x P(x): requires exhibiting just one element where P holds — a single example suffices
Disproving ∀x P(x): requires only one counterexample — find one x where P(x) is false
Disproving ∃x P(x): requires showing P fails for every element — the same heavy burden as proving a ∀

This is the single most exploitable structural fact in the whole topic: ∀ and ∃ have opposite burdens depending on whether you're proving or disproving them. A universal claim is hard to prove, easy to disprove. An existential claim is easy to prove, hard to disprove. Whoever frames the question as ∀ versus ∃ is quietly setting how much work the other side has to do.

Worked Example
Choosing the Right Quantifier Strategically

A regulator wants to claim "every factory in the district complies with the emissions standard" (∀x C(x)). The factory owners' association wants to claim "some factory inspections were conducted unfairly" (∃x U(x)).

Regulator's burden: must demonstrate compliance for every single factory — a complete domain sweep. Missing even one unchecked factory leaves the ∀ claim unproven.

Association's burden: only needs one documented case of an unfair inspection — a single ∃ instantiation closes their argument.

Notice the regulator chose the harder claim to make (∀) because it's the stronger, more useful one if true; the association chose the easier claim to make (∃) because it's all they need to create doubt. Recognising which side is making which type of claim — before evaluating whether they've actually met their burden — is the entire skill this topic trains.

LEC 7
Negation of Quantified Statements
Language First

The negation of "all" is "at least one is not." The negation of "some" is "none." One exception destroys a universal claim. Defeating an existential claim requires showing zero exceptions exist.

¬(∀x P(x)) ≡ ∃x ¬P(x) — at least one fails
¬(∃x P(x)) ≡ ∀x ¬P(x) — all fail

Pattern: quantifier flips · condition negates · both simultaneously
Exam Disguise

PU presents a sentence — "All witnesses testified truthfully" — and asks what the defence must establish. Recognise the ∀ structure before applying the negation rule. The hidden step is the translation, not the mechanics.

Mechanics & Notation
Negation procedure, step by step:
1. Identify the quantifier (∀ or ∃) and the predicate it governs
2. Flip the quantifier (∀↔∃)
3. Negate the predicate (P becomes ¬P)
4. Nothing else changes — the domain stays the same

The two rules are mirror images of each other, which is exactly why they're easy to mix up under time pressure: ¬∀ becomes ∃¬, and ¬∃ becomes ∀¬. Say the rule out loud as "the quantifier flips, the predicate negates" rather than memorising the symbols in isolation — that phrasing survives translation back into English, which is where PU actually tests this.

Worked Example
Negating in Both Directions

"At least one clause in this agreement is unenforceable." Formally: ∃x, C(x) ∧ U(x), where C(x) = "x is a clause in this agreement," U(x) = "x is unenforceable."

Applying the procedure: flip ∃ to ∀, negate the predicate (U becomes ¬U).

Negation: ∀x, C(x) → ¬U(x) — every clause in this agreement is enforceable

Reading it back into English to verify: "for every clause in this agreement, that clause is enforceable" — which is exactly the opposite claim of "at least one is unenforceable," confirming the negation is correct in both directions, not just symbolically derived.

LEC 7
Negation of Universal Conditional Statements
Language First

A universal conditional says: for every x, if condition P holds, then consequence Q follows. To break it, you need one case where P was satisfied and Q did not follow.

Original: ∀x, P(x) → Q(x)
Negation: ∃x, P(x) ∧ ¬Q(x)

Three changes: ∀→∃ · arrow becomes ∧ · Q becomes ¬Q
All three simultaneously. Missing any one is wrong.
The Most Common Error

Students negate Q but forget to flip the quantifier and replace the arrow. Three things change. Every time.

Mechanics & Notation
Checklist for negating ∀x, P(x) → Q(x):
☐ Quantifier changed from ∀ to ∃
☐ Arrow (→) changed to conjunction (∧)
☐ Consequent changed from Q to ¬Q
☐ Antecedent P stays unchanged, unnegated

Notice the antecedent P does not get negated — only the consequent does. This is the easiest piece to get backwards, because the instinct is to negate "everything," but the correct negation keeps P exactly as it was and asserts it actually held while Q failed to follow. That's what makes it a genuine counterexample to the original rule rather than a different rule entirely.

Worked Example
Running the Checklist

"Whenever an employee works overtime, they are entitled to additional pay." Formally: ∀x, O(x) → E(x).

∀ → ∃ ✓
→ becomes ∧ ✓
E(x) becomes ¬E(x) ✓
O(x) stays O(x), unnegated ✓

Negation: ∃x, O(x) ∧ ¬E(x)

In plain English: "there exists at least one employee who worked overtime and was not entitled to additional pay." To disprove the original rule, you need exactly one such employee — someone who genuinely worked overtime (the antecedent held) but received no additional pay (the consequent failed). An employee who simply wasn't entitled to overtime pay because they never worked overtime at all doesn't count — that's a case where the antecedent never triggered, which says nothing about whether the rule itself is true.

Person B attempts to negate it with a counterexample — logically the correct move, but the counterexample's validity depends on whether the antecedent (duress) was genuinely satisfied.

Person C makes the most precise point: if the courts upheld those contracts because duress was not properly established, then Person B's cases do not satisfy P(x) — they are not genuine instances of ∃x P∧¬Q. They are cases where P was absent. The rule is untouched.

This is the distinction between case distinction (C's move) and rule exception (B's move). C operates at a higher level of analytical precision — questioning antecedent satisfaction rather than accepting the framing.

LEC 8
Argument Structure & Validity Testing
Language First

An argument is valid if the conclusion cannot be false when all premises are true — regardless of whether the premises are actually true. An argument is sound if it is valid AND all its premises are actually true.

The standard inference patterns:

Modus Ponens: p → q, p ∴ q ✓ Valid
Modus Tollens: p → q, ¬q ∴ ¬p ✓ Valid
Hypothetical Syllogism: p→q, q→r ∴ p→r ✓ Valid
Disjunctive Syllogism: p∨q, ¬p ∴ q ✓ Valid

Affirming Consequent: p→q, q ∴ p ✗ Invalid
Denying Antecedent: p→q, ¬p ∴ ¬q ✗ Invalid
Mechanics & Notation

Validity-testing procedure, when an argument doesn't obviously match one of the six named patterns:

1. Symbolise every premise and the conclusion
2. Build a truth table covering all variables
3. Find every row where ALL premises are true
4. Check the conclusion in those rows — if it's true in every one, the argument is valid; if false in even one, invalid

The six named patterns are shortcuts that skip this full procedure because their validity (or invalidity) has already been proven once and for all by exactly this method — recognising the pattern lets you skip straight to the answer. When an argument doesn't match a named pattern, the truth-table procedure is the fallback that always works, just slower.

Worked Example
Validity Testing an Unnamed Pattern

"If the application is complete (p), it will be processed (q). The application was not processed (¬q). The application was incomplete (¬p)." — does ¬q, p→q ∴ ¬p hold?

This is Modus Tollens (p→q, ¬q ∴ ¬p) — recognise the named pattern first, which confirms validity instantly. But to verify by table for practice:

Premises true (p→q = T AND ¬q = T) only in row 4. In that row, ¬p = T. The conclusion is true in every row where both premises hold — only one such row exists here, and it confirms the conclusion. Valid.

T2 · DIdentify the named fallacy and explain why the argument is invalid: "If the agreement was signed under duress, it is voidable. This agreement is voidable. Therefore it was signed under duress." What does this argument incorrectly assume about the relationship between the premises?

Pattern: p→q, q ∴ p. Affirming the Consequent — Invalid.

The fallacy: the argument correctly states p→q (duress → voidable) and correctly observes q (the agreement is voidable). But it concludes p (duress) from these two premises — which is the converse of the given conditional, not the conditional itself. Voidability has multiple causes: misrepresentation, undue influence, incapacity, mistake. The fact that the agreement is voidable tells us it might have been signed under duress, but it is consistent with many other explanations.

What the argument incorrectly assumes: that duress is the only way an agreement becomes voidable — i.e., that q→p holds. This would require a biconditional (duress ↔ voidable), not just a one-way conditional. Without that, observing q leaves the cause of voidability entirely open.

(c) Identify the named pattern. (d) A prosecutor argues P3 (motive) is sufficient alone to establish suspicion. Evaluate this claim against P1's structure.

(b) Validity test: from P1 and P2 by modus tollens: ¬S → ¬(A∧M) = ¬A∨¬M. So either the defendant lacked access OR lacked motive (or both). From P3: M=T. Since ¬A∨¬M must hold and ¬M is false (M=T), we need ¬A=T. Therefore ¬A follows. Valid.

(c) Named pattern: Modus Tollens on the compound antecedent, combined with De Morgan's law and elimination of ¬M via P3. This is a two-step chain — not a single named pattern applied once, but a valid multi-step derivation.

(d) Prosecutor's claim — "motive alone establishes suspicion": P1 is (A∧M)→S. This requires both access AND motive. Motive alone (M=T, A unknown) leaves the antecedent A∧M with an unresolved A component. If A=F, then A∧M=F → S is not established. The prosecutor's claim treats M as sufficient, but P1 makes it only necessary-in-conjunction — M is required but not sufficient on its own. This is exactly the "conjunction as sufficient" fallacy applied to P1's antecedent. P1 would need to read M→S (motive alone triggers suspicion) for the prosecutor's claim to be valid, which is a different and weaker trigger than the original rule.

LEC 9
Tautologies, Contradictions & Contingencies
Language First

A tautology is a compound statement that is true in every possible situation — it cannot be made false. A contradiction is false in every situation — it cannot be made true. A contingency is sometimes true, sometimes false — it depends on the facts.

Tautology: p ∨ ¬p — always true ("either raining or not")
Contradiction: p ∧ ¬p — always false ("raining and not raining")
Contingency: p → q — depends on p and q

The practical importance: legal rules stated as tautologies are empty — they carry no information. Rules stated as contradictions are impossible to satisfy. Well-drafted rules are contingencies — they create real conditions that may or may not be satisfied.

Mechanics & Notation
Classification procedure:
1. Build the full truth table for the compound statement
2. Read the final column
All T → tautology  ·  All F → contradiction  ·  Mix of T and F → contingency

This is the same truth-table machinery from Topic 4, applied with a different goal — there you compared two columns for equivalence, here you're checking whether a single column is uniform or mixed. Any compound statement, however complex, reduces to one of exactly three categories once its full table is built; there's no fourth option.

Worked Example
Classifying a Compound Statement by Table

Classify: (p → q) ∨ (q → p)

pqp→qq→p(p→q)∨(q→p)
TTTTT
TFFTT
FTTFT
FFTTT

The final column is T in every row. Tautology. Notice this isn't obvious from looking at the statement alone — "p implies q, or q implies p" sounds like a substantive claim, but at least one direction of implication holds between any two propositions no matter what truth values they take, which is exactly why the table, not intuition, is the reliable test.

Practice Questions
T2 · A Classify each statement as tautology, contradiction, or contingency: (a) "Either the defendant is guilty or the defendant is not guilty." (b) "The defendant is guilty and the defendant is not guilty." (c) "If the defendant confessed, the defendant is guilty."
(a) p ∨ ¬p → Tautology — always true regardless of guilt
(b) p ∧ ¬p → Contradiction — impossible, cannot be simultaneously guilty and not guilty
(c) p → q → Contingency — true unless confession occurred without guilt (e.g. false confession)

Legal note on (c): A confession is strong evidence but not logically sufficient proof of guilt — false confessions occur. The statement is a contingency, not a tautology, because a world where someone confessed but was genuinely not guilty is logically possible.

T3 · A A legislative drafting error produces this clause: "A person is liable if they caused harm or they did not cause harm." A second clause reads: "A person is not liable if they both complied with regulations and did not comply with regulations." Identify the logical type of each clause, explain the practical legal consequence, and propose corrected drafting for each.

Clause 1: "Liable if H ∨ ¬H" — the condition is p ∨ ¬p, a tautology. It is always true, meaning the liability condition is always satisfied. Everyone is always liable under this clause. Practical consequence: Universal liability with no limiting condition — the clause is legally meaningless as a standard because it applies to everyone unconditionally.

Clause 2: "Not liable if C ∧ ¬C" — the condition is p ∧ ¬p, a contradiction. It is never true, meaning the exemption condition can never be satisfied. No one can ever qualify for this exemption. Practical consequence: A dead letter exemption — it exists in the statute but cannot benefit anyone.

Corrected drafting:

Clause 1: "A person is liable if they caused harm."
H → Liable (contingency — applies only when harm occurred)

Clause 2: "A person is not liable if they complied with regulations."
C → ¬Liable (contingency — exemption available when compliance is shown)
Tautology and contradiction in statutory drafting · practical legal consequences of each · corrective drafting as application of contingency · Tier 3 because it requires identifying the error, explaining the consequence, and producing corrected language

Spotting these on a contract or statute review is a direct, billable application of this exact classification skill.

LEC 10
Integrated Logic — Full Argument Analysis
Language First

This topic integrates everything from Lec. 1–9 into full argument analysis. The skill is: read a complex argument, identify its logical skeleton, test its validity, identify any fallacies, and evaluate what evidence would strengthen or weaken it.

The process in four steps:

1. Identify the conclusion — what is the argument trying to establish?
2. Identify the premises — what reasons are given?
3. Identify the logical form — what inference pattern connects them?
4. Test validity — if premises true, must conclusion follow?
If not: name the flaw.
Mechanics & Notation

Integrated arguments are rarely a single named pattern — they're usually a chain of two or three patterns stacked together, sometimes with an unstated premise holding the chain together.

Finding an implicit premise: if the stated premises don't logically reach the conclusion on their own, ask what additional statement, if assumed true, would close the gap. That missing statement is the implicit premise — and in legal argument, it's usually the most contestable part of the whole structure, precisely because it was never said out loud.

Finding the weakest link: in a multi-premise chain, the argument is only as strong as its least defensible premise — locate the one premise that, if false, breaks the entire conclusion, and that's where a challenge should be aimed, not at premises that are already secure.
Worked Example
Full Four-Step Analysis

"Every licensed pharmacist must complete continuing education. Person D has not completed continuing education this year. Continuing education requirements only apply to active licence holders. Therefore Person D's licence must be inactive."

Step 1 — conclusion: Person D's licence is inactive.

Step 2 — premises: P1: ∀x, active(x) → must_CE(x). P2: ¬CE(D). P3 (restates P1's scope): CE requirement applies only to active licence holders.

Step 3 — form: P1 + P2 looks like it should give Modus Tollens (¬must_CE(D) → ¬active(D))but P2 actually says ¬CE(D), not ¬must_CE(D) — not completing CE is not the same as not being required to.

Step 4 — validity: Invalid as stated. The argument conflates "did not complete CE" with "was not required to complete CE." A licensed, active pharmacist who simply failed to comply with the requirement is indistinguishable, on this evidence alone, from one whose licence was never active in the first place. The conclusion needs an additional premise — evidence that Person D's non-compliance was due to inapplicability of the rule, not violation of it — which the argument as given does not supply.

Practice Questions
T2 · A Analyse this argument: "All regulated industries must file annual compliance reports. This company is in a regulated industry. Yet it has not filed a compliance report. Therefore this company is in violation." Identify the form, assess validity, and identify what additional premise, if any, is needed.
P1: ∀x, regulated(x) → must_file(x)
P2: regulated(Company) = T

From P1 + P2 by Modus Ponens: must_file(Company) = T. Combined with P3 (¬filed): the company was required to file and did not. The conclusion follows — but requires one implicit premise: failing to do what is required constitutes a violation. This is reasonable and likely definitional, but should be stated explicitly for the argument to be fully sound.

T3 · A A senior advocate argues: "No person should be punished twice for the same offence. This accused has already served a sentence for this act. Some argue a new statute creates a separate offence from the same act. But an offence that arises from the same act is the same offence. Therefore the new prosecution is barred." Reconstruct the full logical form, identify all premises (stated and implicit), assess validity, and identify the weakest link an opposing counsel should target.
P1: ∀x, ∀o, punished(x,o) → ¬should_punish_again(x,o) [double jeopardy rule]
P2: punished(Accused, act_A) = T [served sentence]
P3: new_offence arises_from act_A [prosecution's basis]
P4 (implicit): ∀o, arises_from(o, act) → same_offence(o, act) [advocate's key claim]
C: ¬should_prosecute(Accused) = T

Validity: If all premises including P4 are true, the conclusion follows by a chain of universal instantiation and modus ponens. The argument is logically valid given P4.

The weakest link — P4. The advocate's implicit premise that "an offence arising from the same act is the same offence" is the entire weight-bearing beam of the argument. Opposing counsel should target it directly: different statutes may create distinct offences from the same act if they protect different legal interests, require different elements, or address different harms. This is the standard double jeopardy analysis in most jurisdictions — the Blockburger test (US) asks whether each offence requires proof of a fact the other does not, not merely whether they arise from the same act.

Strategic implication: The prosecution should argue that P4 misstates the legal test, replacing "same act" with "same elements" as the correct criterion. This defeats the argument without challenging any of the other premises.
